CPP = MAP − ICP
Key Formula
CPP = MAP − ICP

Cerebral Perfusion Pressure = Mean Arterial Pressure − Intracranial Pressure
⬆ ICP or ⬇ MAP → ⬇ CPP → Brain ischemia 🚨

🔑 Monro-Kellie Doctrine — "Brain Box Rule"

The skull is a fixed rigid box. It contains: Brain tissue (80%), Blood (10%), CSF (10%). If ONE increases, another MUST decrease to compensate. When compensation fails → ICP rises.

🚨 Cushing's Triad = LATE SIGN = MEDICAL EMERGENCY → Brain Herniation Imminent!
EARLY ICP Signs LATE ICP Signs (Cushing's Triad)
✓ Headache 🚨 Bradycardia
✓ Restlessness 🚨 Widened Pulse Pressure (SBP↑, DBP stable)
✓ Confusion 🚨 Irregular/Cheyne-Stokes Respirations
✓ Decreased LOC (EARLIEST sign!) 🚨 Posturing (Decorticate → Decerebrate)
✓ Pupillary changes begin 🚨 Fixed, dilated pupils
💡 NCLEX Rule: LATE signs ALWAYS win priority! See Cushing's Triad → See patient FIRST!

✅ ICP-REDUCING Interventions

  • HOB 30° — promotes venous drainage
  • Neutral head/neck position — no flexion
  • Limit stimulation — no clustering care
  • Quiet environment, dim lights
  • Mannitol (osmotic diuretic) IV
  • Hypertonic saline
  • Hyperventilation (short-term CO₂ ↓)

🚫 Things That ↑ ICP — AVOID!

  • Coughing / sneezing / Valsalva
  • Bending over / hip flexion
  • Straining during BM
  • Neck flexion or rotation
  • Clustering nursing tasks
  • Holding breath during movement
  • High-pitched noise / bright lights
💡 TEACHING TRAP: "I can bend over to pick things up" → WRONG! "I can hold my breath when moving" → WRONG! Both increase ICP!

Seizures
Included in ~20 cognition questions
Phase What Happens Nurse Actions
AURA (Pre-ictal) Strange smell, visual flashes, sudden fear, dizziness — warning signs BEFORE seizure Ensure safety, document the aura type
TONIC Phase Muscle stiffening, LOC, rigid posture, may cry out Note start time ⏱, protect head, side-lying
CLONIC Phase Rhythmic jerking movements, muscle contractions Continue protection, loosen clothing
POSTICTAL Confusion, sleepiness, headache, amnesia of event Reorient, assess neuro, document duration
🚨 Status Epilepticus = Seizure >5 min OR two seizures without recovery → EMERGENCY → IV Lorazepam/Diazepam STAT

✅ Seizure Safety — DO These

  • Turn client on their SIDE (prevent aspiration)
  • Protect the HEAD from injury
  • Note the START TIME — duration matters
  • Loosen tight clothing
  • Oxygen + suction at bedside
  • Padded side rails UP (lowest position)
  • IV access maintained for medications

🚫 NEVER Do These!

  • NEVER insert tongue blade or anything in mouth
  • NEVER restrain the client
  • NEVER put bed in HIGH position
  • NEVER leave client alone
  • NEVER hold the client down

💡 Seizure NCLEX Trap!

Any answer involving the mouth = WRONG. Nothing goes IN the mouth. No tongue blade. No fingers. No bite block in acute seizure.

Any answer involving restraints = WRONG.
Correct answer almost always involves SIDE-LYING + PROTECT HEAD + TIME IT.

Stroke
Included in cognition section
Ischemic Stroke Hemorrhagic Stroke
Blood clot blocks artery Blood vessel ruptures
Most common (87%) Less common, MORE deadly
tPA eligible (if within 4.5hr, no bleed) tPA CONTRAINDICATED 🚨
Risk: A-fib, atherosclerosis, smoking Risk: HTN #1, cocaine, aneurysm, AVM

🧠 FAST Assessment — Stroke Recognition

FFacial drooping — one side droops
AArm weakness — one arm drifts down
SSpeech difficulty — slurred or garbled
TTime — call 911 IMMEDIATELY!
🚨 PRIORITY: Unknown time of symptom onset = #1 priority. TIME determines treatment eligibility for tPA!
⚠️ TEACHING: "I'll wait to see if it goes away" → WRONG! NEVER wait with stroke symptoms! Every minute = brain cells dying!

Hemorrhagic Stroke Risk Factors

  • Hypertension — #1 cause 🚨
  • Cocaine / amphetamine use
  • Cerebral aneurysm (can rupture)
  • AV malformation (AVM)
  • Anticoagulant overuse

Stroke Symptoms to Know

  • Sudden vision loss (one or both eyes)
  • Arm/leg weakness (one side)
  • Facial droop
  • Slurred / garbled speech
  • Balance problems / sudden fall
  • Sudden severe headache ("thunderclap")

Pharmacology — Anticonvulsants & ICP Drugs
~6 pharm adverse effects questions

Phenytoin

(Dilantin)
Anticonvulsant
🚨 IV rate: MAX 50 mg/min. Causes dysrhythmias!
Classic side effectGingival hyperplasia (gum overgrowth)
Toxicity signsNystagmus, ataxia, diplopia
IV riskDysrhythmias, hypotension
MonitorSerum levels, cardiac rhythm
💡 Gingival hyperplasia = always phenytoin on NCLEX!

Lamotrigine

(Lamictal)
Anticonvulsant / Mood Stabilizer
🚨 Rash → can progress to Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS) — FATAL!
Stop ifANY rash appears — report immediately
MonitorLiver function, skin changes
SJS signsBlistering, mucous membrane involvement
💡 Rash = STOP lamotrigine = call provider NOW

Levetiracetam

(Keppra)
Anticonvulsant
Psych side effectsMood changes, aggression, depression
ReportAny behavioral/mood changes IMMEDIATELY
OtherDizziness, sedation, infection risk
💡 Keppra = "Keppra Rage" — mood changes are serious!

Valproic Acid

(Depakote)
Anticonvulsant / Mood Stabilizer
🚨 Hepatotoxicity + Pancreatitis risk!
MonitorLFTs, amylase/lipase, CBC
Signs of pancreatitisAbdominal pain, nausea, vomiting
Also watchRash, edema, thrombocytopenia
TeratogenicCategory D — avoid in pregnancy

Phenobarbital

(Luminal)
Barbiturate / Anticonvulsant
🚨 Respiratory depression — especially when combined with other CNS depressants!
Priority monitorRespiratory rate and depth
Also monitorHypotension, sedation, delirium
CNS effectsSedation, confusion
💡 Resp depression = most dangerous side effect

Mannitol

(Osmitrol)
Osmotic Diuretic — ICP Drug
GoalReduce ICP by pulling fluid out of brain
Expected outcomes↑ Urine output, ↓ ICP, ↓ cerebral edema
Intervention neededCrackles, weight gain, fluid overload, HF signs
MonitorElectrolytes, osmolality, urine output
🚨 Crackles after mannitol = pulmonary edema = EMERGENCY!
⚠️ GENERAL ANTICONVULSANT TEACHING: NEVER stop medication suddenly → can trigger breakthrough seizures / status epilepticus. Report any rash IMMEDIATELY.

Tissue Integrity
~20 questions on exam

Wound Healing — Delay Factors

  • Infection / contamination
  • Poor nutrition (low protein/zinc/Vit C)
  • Diabetes / poor glycemic control
  • Poor circulation / peripheral vascular disease
  • Immunosuppression / steroids
  • Smoking — reduces oxygenation
  • Age — slower cellular repair

Wound Infection Signs

  • Redness, warmth, swelling (classic)
  • Purulent / odorous drainage
  • Increased pain at wound site
  • Fever, elevated WBC
  • Dehiscence or evisceration 🚨

Pressure Injury — Risk Factors

  • Immobility — #1 risk
  • Incontinence / moisture
  • Malnutrition / low albumin
  • Decreased sensation
  • Shear / friction forces
  • Braden Scale — assesses risk

Skin Integrity Nursing

  • Turn/reposition q2h
  • Moisture barriers for incontinence
  • Adequate nutrition — protein!
  • Pressure-relieving mattresses
  • Avoid dragging patient
  • Keep skin clean and dry
💡 Aspiration Risk: Always assess swallowing before feeding. HOB 30–45° during tube feedings. Check tube placement before each feed!

Grief, Loss & Palliative Care
~17 questions on exam
Kübler-Ross Stage What Client May Say/Do Nursing Response
1. Denial "This can't be happening" / Seeking other opinions Allow denial — it's protective. Do NOT force acceptance.
2. Anger "Why me?" / Lashing out at staff or family Do NOT take personally. Allow expression. Therapeutic communication.
3. Bargaining "If I can just live to see my grandchild..." Listen actively. Support spiritual needs.
4. Depression Withdrawal, crying, loss of interest, sadness Presence therapy. Encourage verbalization. Support network.
5. Acceptance "I'm at peace." / Planning end of life Support planning. Involve family. Comfort measures.
💡 Stages are NOT linear! Clients move back and forth. Never tell a client what stage they "should" be in.

Palliative Care Principles

  • Focus: QUALITY of life — not cure
  • Appropriate at any stage of serious illness
  • Symptom management is priority
  • Includes pain, dyspnea, nausea, anxiety
  • Involves patient, family, interdisciplinary team

Hospice Care

  • Comfort-focused — curative treatment stopped
  • Prognosis: 6 months or less
  • Can continue in home setting
  • Family receives bereavement support
  • DNR typically but not required

End-of-Life Client Needs

  • Pain and symptom control
  • Fear of the dying process
  • Loss of control and independence
  • Financial and family concerns
  • Spiritual and cultural support
  • Dignity in dying

Therapeutic Communication — EOL

  • Active listening — silence is okay
  • Open-ended questions
  • Avoid clichés ("Everything happens for a reason")
  • Reflect feelings back
  • Encourage expression of fears
  • Respect cultural/spiritual beliefs

Top NCLEX Test-Taking Tips

🚨 Late Signs Win Priority

When choosing WHO to see first, LATE/severe signs ALWAYS beat early signs. Cushing's triad? See that patient immediately, no matter what.

📉 "Further Teaching" Questions

Find the WRONG answer the client says. Look for the statement that INCREASES ICP, INCREASES stroke risk, or goes against safety protocols.

💊 Rash = Stop + Call

Any anticonvulsant + rash mentioned = ALWAYS answer "report rash immediately." Lamotrigine especially → SJS risk.

⏰ Stroke = Time First

Priority with stroke is ALWAYS establishing time of onset. This determines tPA eligibility. Unknown time = immediate concern.

🔇 LOC = First Sign

Decreased level of consciousness is the EARLIEST sign of neurological deterioration. When you see it as an option, select it for "earliest."

🏥 ABCs First, Always

Airway → Breathing → Circulation. Irregular respirations (airway/breathing) always win over cardiovascular or behavioral symptoms.
