Overview & Learning Objectives

What you need to know, why it matters, and how it connects to nursing practice.

🧠 What is Cognition?

Cognition = the act of thinking through the knowledge and understanding that is acquired. It represents a fundamental human feature that distinguishes living from merely existing.

When cognition is impaired, the impact radiates across every dimension of a person's life:

🧠 Psychological 💪 Physical 👥 Social ✨ Spiritual
📖 Neurocognitive Disorders

Neurocognitive disorders = alterations in cognitive functioning due to underlying physiological changes caused by brain pathology.

⚡ Delirium — Acute, reversible, sudden onset
🧩 Dementia — Chronic, progressive, slow onset

DSM-V renames dementia → Neurocognitive Disorder (mild or major)

⚠️
Critical Distinction You Must Know Delirium and dementia can look similar but are NOT the same. Delirium is ACUTE and often REVERSIBLE. Dementia is CHRONIC and PROGRESSIVE. You must distinguish between them — confusing the two can lead to missed treatments, permanent brain damage, or inappropriate care.

Delirium — Acute Confusion

Acute, often reversible cognitive disturbance. Treat the cause → resolve the symptoms.

⚡ Core Definition

Delirium = acute cognitive disturbance that is often reversible, with acute onset. It is a constellation of symptoms, not a single disorder. Without treatment, delirium can cause permanent brain damage.

⚡ Signs & Symptoms

  • Inability to direct, focus, sustain, and shift attention
  • Abrupt onset with fluctuating clinical features (periods of lucidity)
  • Disorganized thinking
  • Disorientation (often to time and place)
  • Anxiety and agitation
  • Poor memory
  • Hallucinations (at times)

Medical Conditions That Cause Delirium

🦠 Infectious / Systemic
  • Infection — systemic sepsis
  • Hepatic disease
  • Renal disease
⚗️ Metabolic / Nutritional
  • Metabolic disorders (hypoxia, glucose)
  • Fluid & electrolyte imbalance
  • Thiamine deficiency
  • Dehydration
💊 Medications
  • Analgesics, anticonvulsants
  • Neuroleptics
  • Sedatives/hypnotics
  • Substance withdrawal (alcohol, sedatives)
  • Substance intoxication
  • Geriatric polypharmacy
🧠 Neurological
  • Head trauma
  • Seizure activity
  • Post-op complications
🏥 Environmental / Other
  • Immobilization
  • Sleep deprivation
  • Sensory impairment (hearing, vision)
  • Restraint use
  • Environmental noise, lack of orienting material
  • 45% of elderly develop delirium after hip fracture

🔬 Delirium Assessment

📊 Assessment Components
  • Mental, neurological, physical examination
  • Determine client's baseline mental status (ask a family member)
  • Review medications — drug interactions, polypharmacy
  • Toxicity screening
  • Laboratory values
  • Urinalysis
  • Diagnostic imaging (X-ray, CT, MRI)
💡
Clinical Insight: Know the Baseline Always establish the patient's baseline cognitive status before assuming current behavior is new or abnormal. A family member is your best resource for this. A sudden change from baseline = investigate!
🚨
Untreated Delirium = Permanent Brain Damage Delirium is a medical emergency. Treat the underlying cause immediately. Symptoms are fully reversible when treated appropriately — but only if caught in time.

🩺 Nursing Interventions for Delirium

🛡️ Ensure Safe Environment
  • Free from fall hazards
  • IV lines out of sight, nontangled
  • Keep belongings near (eyeglasses, call light)
  • Adequate lighting
  • Bed alarm; bed in low position
  • Avoid restraints — worsen agitation!
  • Approach slowly, from the front
🤝 Support & Supervision
  • Monitor neuro status & VS frequently
  • Calm, reassuring approach — simple sentences
  • ADLs: food, water, toileting
  • Family or caregiver/sitter present
  • Encourage familiar objects from home
  • Maintain daily routine
  • Low stimulation environment
  • Reorient gently and realistically
  • Encourage normal sleep-wake cycle
🔍 Address Underlying Cause
  • Identify and treat infection, metabolic imbalances
  • Review and adjust medications
  • Ensure adequate hydration and nutrition (easy-to-eat foods)
  • PRN medication for severe anxiety with caution — benzos used cautiously

🚫 Critical Medication Safety — Delirium & Benzos

Benzodiazepines (e.g., lorazepam) can cause reverse effects in the elderly — meaning they may paradoxically worsen agitation, confusion, and behavioral symptoms rather than calming them. In the elderly with delirium:

  • Benzos are used with extreme caution and only for severe anxiety/agitation
  • Benzos are NOT first-line treatment for delirium
  • Nonpharmacologic approaches are ALWAYS attempted first
  • A recently prescribed benzo (like lorazepam) may actually be contributing to the delirium
✨
Communication Approach for Delirium Approach patient slowly from the front. Address by name only — NOT "Honey," "Sweetie," or "Buddy." Use simple, direct statements. Well-lit environment. Low stimulation. Provide a clock and calendar in the room for reorientation.

Dementia — Alzheimer's Disease

Chronic, progressive, irreversible — but manageable with the right nursing care.

🧩 Core Definition

Dementia = deterioration and global impairment of cognitive functioning that is chronic, usually progressive but slow. Alzheimer's disease (AD) is the most common cause of dementia.

No Known Cure Irreversible Progressive Diagnosis of Exclusion

📊 Alzheimer's Statistics

  • 6th leading cause of death in the US (2016, CDC)
  • 5.8 million cases in the US
  • 1 in 3 seniors dies with Alzheimer's or another dementia
  • Expected to rise to 14 million in US by 2050
  • Over 16 million Americans provide unpaid care to people with AD
  • 1 in 8 people under age 65 have AD (Alzheimer's Association)
  • Symptoms usually appear after age 60; average duration: 8 years

⚠️ Risk Factors

  • Increasing age (most symptoms appear after 60)
  • Genetic factors
  • Cardiovascular disease (diet, exercise modifiable)
  • Head injury and traumatic brain injury
  • Other types of dementia: vascular, AIDS-related, chronic substance abuse, degenerative neuro disorders, post-ICU
🛡️
Prevention Strategies Good nutrition (↓ inflammation, oxidative stress, lower glucose and cholesterol), folic acid/B-6/B-12 to reduce homocysteine, social relationships, intellectual stimulation, exercise, and reducing head trauma risk (helmets, seat belts).

🔍 Diagnosis of Exclusion

DSM-V replaces the term "dementia" with neurocognitive disorder (mild or major). Diagnosis requires:

  • Memory impairment PLUS one of: Aphasia, Apraxia, Agnosia, or Disturbance in executive functioning
  • Significant cognitive decline from baseline in: complex attention, executive function, learning and memory, language, perceptual-motor skills, or social cognition
  • Deficits interfere with independence in everyday activities
  • Does NOT occur exclusively in the context of delirium
  • NOT explained by other mental disorders (pseudodementia)
🎭
Pseudodementia Depression in the elderly can MIMIC dementia — this is called pseudodementia. Watch for: memory loss, ↓ thinking/learning, fatigue, irritability, somatic complaints, changes in appetite/sleep. Key: treat the underlying depression, and cognitive symptoms may resolve!

📈 Stages of Alzheimer's Disease

Text uses 7 stages, but progression is essentially: Early → Middle → End stage

🌱 Early Stage (Mild)
  • Memory loss (recent)
  • Disorientation
  • Cannot learn new information
  • Limited problem solving
  • Trouble with in-depth conversation; forgets common words
  • Personality changes: listless, apathetic, sudden temper
  • Easily fatigued
  • Wants familiar people, places, things
  • Still able to care for self — no supervision needed yet
  • Family may not realize something is wrong
⚠️ Middle Stage (Moderate)
  • Unstable emotions: anger, paranoia
  • Emotional disinhibition
  • Trouble eating; childlike behavior
  • Cannot perform self-care without assistance
  • Internalized, insensitive to others
  • Pacing and agitation
  • Bowel and bladder incontinence
  • Wandering
  • Visual hallucinations and delusions
  • Difficulty recognizing family/friends
  • Meaningless words/actions (rummaging)
  • Catastrophic reactions; Sundowning syndrome
🔴 End Stage (Severe)
  • Global memory loss (long-term AND short-term)
  • Significant weight loss; needs help eating
  • Verbal communication limited to guttural sounds or mutism
  • Total loss of voluntary bowel and bladder control
  • Bedridden (fetal position)
  • Profound neurological loss (seizures)
  • Contractures, pressure ulcers, systemic infections
  • Death from: heart failure, pneumonia, kidney failure, starvation

Pathophysiology

Understanding the "why" behind what you see in the patient.

🧠 Neurobiology of Alzheimer's Disease

Two essential neurotransmitters implicated in Alzheimer's disease are acetylcholine and glutamate.

🔗 Acetylcholine Pathway

Acetylcholine is involved with learning, memory, and mood.

In Alzheimer's: As disease progresses, the brain produces LESS acetylcholine. What little remains is rapidly destroyed by the enzyme acetylcholinesterase (AChE).
Treatment logic: Cholinesterase inhibitors keep the AChE enzyme from breaking down acetylcholine → increasing the level and duration of action of the neurotransmitter.
⚡ Glutamate Pathway

Glutamate is involved with cell signaling, learning, and memory.

In Alzheimer's: Glutamate binds to NMDA receptors and allows calcium to enter the cell. Excess glutamate and chronic overexposure to calcium leads to damaged cells.
Treatment logic: NMDA antagonists (memantine) block NMDA receptors → reduce excess calcium influx → less neurotoxicity.

🔬 Structural Brain Changes in Alzheimer's

🪨 Amyloid Plaques

Sticky clumps found between nerve cells that may cause or result from the disease. They block communication at synapses, activate immune cells, trigger inflammation, and devour healthy microtubules and disabled cells.

🧵 Neurofibrillary Tangles

Abnormal collections of protein threads inside nerve cells, composed mainly of a protein called tau. Tangles disrupt the transport of food molecules, cell parts, and other key elements → cell death.

🧠 Brain Atrophy

The cerebral cortex shrinks up, damaging areas involved in thinking, planning, and remembering. The hippocampus (essential for memory) undergoes severe shrinkage. Ventricles (fluid-filled spaces) grow larger.

💡
The Connection: Pathophysiology → Medications → Nursing Care Understanding what is happening in the brain tells you WHY the patient behaves the way they do AND why certain medications work (or don't). Cholinesterase inhibitors help because acetylcholine is depleted. NMDA antagonists help because glutamate overexcites damaged neurons. Neither cures the disease — they slow progression.

The behavioral symptoms you see in dementia/delirium are not random — they are rooted in physical processes in the brain and body.

🔑 Critical Nursing Insight: Behavior = Communication

When a patient with cognitive impairment is agitated, wandering, screaming, or refusing care — they are NOT being mean-spirited. They are attempting to express feelings and needs they cannot verbalize. Ask yourself:

  • Is there pain, discomfort, or a physical need unmet?
  • Is the environment overwhelming (noise, light, strangers)?
  • Has routine been disrupted?
  • Is there a medication contributing to the behavior?
  • Is this new behavior — meaning possible superimposed delirium?

Pharmacology of Cognition Disorders

Medications for Alzheimer's, dementia-related behavioral symptoms, and delirium management.

CLASS 1: Cholinesterase Inhibitors (CEIs)

Donepezil (Aricept)
Cholinesterase Inhibitor — Drug of Choice for All Stages
⚙️ Mechanism

Inhibits acetylcholinesterase (AChE) enzyme from inactivating acetylcholine → increases available ACh at synapses → improves cholinergic transmission involved in memory and cognition.

✅ Therapeutic Effects
  • Helps slow deterioration in cognitive functioning
  • Only marginally effective overall
  • Minimal benefits after one year
⚠️ Side Effects
  • GI effects most common: nausea, vomiting, diarrhea
  • Risk of side effects doubles in people over age 85
  • Anorexia, weight loss
💊 Nursing Notes
  • ODT tablet melts on tongue — follow with water
  • Lower starting dose: 5 mg/day
  • Approved for all stages of Alzheimer's
  • Monitor GI side effects; assess weight
Rivastigmine (Exelon) & Galantamine (Razadyne)
Cholinesterase Inhibitors — Additional Agents
⚙️ Mechanism

Same class as donepezil — inhibit AChE to preserve acetylcholine. Rivastigmine: mild to moderate stage. Galantamine: mild to moderate stage. Rivastigmine also available as transdermal patch (Exelon Patch).

💊 Nursing Notes
  • Common GI side effects (same class)
  • Exelon patch: rotate site, monitor skin integrity
  • Temporary improvement only; does not halt progression
  • Benefits are modest; reassess continuously

CLASS 2: NMDA Receptor Antagonist

Memantine (Namenda)
NMDA Receptor Antagonist — Moderate to Severe Alzheimer's Only
⚙️ Mechanism

Blocks the effects of excess glutamate at NMDA receptors, which restores signal transmission. Reduces neurotoxicity caused by excess calcium influx from overactive NMDA receptors.

✅ Therapeutic Effects
  • If started during moderate (not end) stage, may temporarily delay disease progression
  • Can be used with cholinesterase inhibitors (complementary)
  • Typically added AFTER trying CEIs first
⚠️ Side Effects
  • Dizziness
  • Headaches
  • Confusion (ironically)
🚨 Cautions
  • Should NOT be used for mild dementia
  • Use only for moderate to severe stages
  • Not a cure — slows progression temporarily

CLASS 3: Behavioral / Symptom-Management Medications

🚨
CRITICAL: Antipsychotics in Dementia — Know This for NCLEX & Practice DO NOT administer haloperidol (Haldol) in clients with dementia-related psychosis — there is an increased incidence of death. Antipsychotics are NOT FDA-approved for dementia-related behavioral symptoms and carry a black box warning for increased mortality in elderly patients with dementia. If antipsychotics must be considered: Risperdal (risperidone) is better than Haldol; Seroquel (quetiapine) is slightly better than Risperdal.
⚠️ Use With Caution
  • Antipsychotics — NOT approved; use with extreme caution only if nonpharmacological fails; Seroquel > Risperdal > Haldol (safety hierarchy)
  • Antidepressants — Can treat comorbid depression/anxiety
  • Anticonvulsants — Sometimes used for behavioral symptoms
🚫 Avoid / Contraindicated
  • Benzodiazepines (e.g., lorazepam, clorazepate) — Avoid in elderly with dementia; worsen confusion and disorientation; can paradoxically increase agitation
  • Haldol (haloperidol) — Increased mortality in dementia-related psychosis
  • Some sleeping pills — may have reverse effects in the elderly

💊 Nursing Pharmacology Priorities — Ask These Questions

  • Is the medication helping or harming? Assess cognition, behavior, and function before and after medication changes
  • Does the benefit outweigh the risk? CEIs are only marginally effective; double-check risk in patients over 85
  • Is any current medication CAUSING the cognitive symptoms? Review all meds for anticholinergic effects, sedatives, benzos
  • Is this medication appropriate for the stage? Memantine = moderate-severe only; do NOT start in mild dementia
  • Nonpharmacologic always first for agitation — communication, environment, reassurance before reaching for meds

Nursing Care & Clinical Management

Safety, communication, family support, and holistic patient-centered care.

🌟 The 4 C's of Dementia Care

C
Calm

Slow voice, gentle approach, low stimulation

C
Clear

Simple, direct statements; no complex instructions

C
Consistent

Structured routine; same approach every time

C
Caring

Dignity, empathy, creativity, and flexibility

🧠
Core Principle "Remember the disease causes the behavior." Always treat the person as an adult. Use creativity and flexibility. Recognize that the patient's experience of reality may be different from yours — and that's okay.
✅
Functional Independence Assist participation in ADLs at the highest level of functional ability. Doing everything "for" the patient may seem kind but actually accelerates functional decline. Let them try — even if it takes longer.

🗣️ Communication Techniques

✅ DO These Things
  • Address the patient by name only (not "Honey," "Sweetie," "Buddy")
  • Approach slowly and from the front — never from behind
  • Simplify speech — short, clear, direct statements
  • Use gestures, visual and verbal clues
  • Speak in a normal tone of voice
  • Observe facial expression and body language
  • Explore reasons for behavior
  • Remember refusal may mean forgotten task or interrupted routine
  • Provide structure and routine daily
  • Use creativity and flexibility
🚫 AVOID These Things
  • Do NOT argue or confront
  • Do NOT quiz the patient
  • Do NOT approach from behind while speaking
  • Do NOT over-stimulate
  • Do NOT tell the person they're "not trying hard enough"
  • Do NOT use commands
  • Do NOT ask "why" questions
  • Do NOT use condescending names or baby talk
  • Disregard unkind personal comments — the disease speaks, not the person

🛡️ Safety Priorities

🚨 SAFETY IS ALWAYS PRIORITY #1

  • Fall prevention: clear clutter, remove area rugs, night lights in bathroom/bedroom/hallway, bed alarm, low bed position
  • Wandering prevention: door locks that cannot be easily opened, Safe Return Program enrollment
  • Medication safety: supervise medication administration — do NOT allow self-management in moderate/severe stage
  • Avoid restraints — worsen agitation and can cause injury
  • Environmental safety: well-lit, hazard-free, low stimulation
  • IV lines: out of sight, nontangled (for hospitalized patients)
  • Nutrition: ensure adequate food and fluid intake — check weight
  • Skin integrity: pressure ulcer prevention in late stages

🎭 Validation Therapy vs. Reality Orientation

🎭 Validation Therapy

Enter the patient's reality rather than correcting it. Used when reorientation to the present would be cruel and result in further agitation.

Goals:

  • Help people resolve unfinished business
  • Help people die in peace
  • Reduce anxiety; increase self-worth
  • Prevent withdrawal and vegetation
  • Provide joy for the caregiver
  • Can lead to increased communication and behavior change
🕐 When to Use Which
Use Reality Orientation when: Patient is delirious and needs reorientation. The goal is to safely ground them in time/place. Always gentle and reassuring.
Use Validation Therapy when: Patient is in mid/late dementia. Correcting them about deceased loved ones or past realities causes distress and agitation with no benefit. Enter their world.
💡
Example: A patient insists she needs to pick up her children from school (children are grown adults). Correcting her would cause grief and agitation. Instead: "Tell me about your children." This validates her and reduces anxiety.

💕 Caring for the Caregiver

Caregiver burden is real, common, and emotionally challenging. Support groups, respite care, and community resources can provide relief without removing caregiving responsibilities.

Resources to Offer

  • Therapeutic communication — elicit caregiver concerns
  • Respite care (temporary relief from caregiving)
  • Community resources and support groups
  • Financial and legal affairs guidance
  • Address guilt over nursing home placement

Best Nurse Responses

  • "I understand. Have you considered respite care or a support group?"
  • Acknowledge emotional difficulty without judgment
  • Validate caregiver's feelings of being overwhelmed
  • Connect to Alzheimer's Association: alz.org

📚 Patient & Family Education

  • There is no cure for Alzheimer's — medications slow progression, not reverse it
  • Donepezil (CEI) does NOT stop the disease — it temporarily slows cognitive decline
  • Behavioral symptoms are caused by the disease process in the brain — not intentional behavior
  • Establish a structured, predictable daily routine — this reduces confusion and anxiety
  • Safe Return Program: enroll the patient; ID bracelet for wandering safety
  • Modify home environment: remove clutter, install night lights, secure exits
  • Avoid triggers you know cause agitation
  • When to call the provider: sudden worsening of confusion (may indicate delirium), new falls, medication side effects
  • Caregiver self-care is NOT selfish — it is essential to sustaining care quality

📋 Case Study 1: Mrs. Smith — Delirium

83-year-old female | Pneumonia + Dehydration

Clinical Scenario

Mrs. Smith is an 83-year-old woman admitted from a skilled nursing facility for pneumonia and dehydration. Her daughter reports increasing confusion over the past two days. She is disoriented to time and place, frequently attempting to get out of bed despite weakness. Speech is incoherent at times; she appears agitated, picking at her sheets. PMH: hypertension, osteoarthritis, mild cognitive impairment. Current meds: lisinopril, acetaminophen, lorazepam (recently prescribed for anxiety).

Vital Signs & Assessment
T 101.3°F ↑ HR 102 bpm ↑ BP 130/78 mmHg RR 24 ↑ SpO₂ 94% on 2L NC ↓

Neuro: Disoriented to time/place, unable to follow complex commands, fluctuating attention, restless, hallucinations

Labs: WBC 14,500 (↑ elevated), Na⁺ 130 mEq/L (↓ low), BUN 28 mg/dL (↑ elevated), Cr 1.4

NCLEX Questions
Q1 · Single AnswerWhich factor most likely contributed to Mrs. Smith's delirium?
✅ Correct: B — Pneumonia and dehydration. Infection and dehydration are the most common acute causes of delirium in older adults. They trigger metabolic imbalances that disrupt brain function. Note: the recent lorazepam prescription is also a contributing red flag — benzos can cause paradoxical confusion in the elderly.
SATAWhich nursing interventions are most appropriate to ensure Mrs. Smith's safety? Select all that apply.
✅ Correct: C, I, J
✅ C — A sitter or family member provides supervision and prevents falls.
✅ I — Call light within reach supports patient safety and autonomy.
✅ J — Treating the infection (azithromycin) addresses the underlying cause of delirium.
❌ A — Restraints worsen agitation and increase fall/injury risk.
❌ B — Adequate lighting is needed; darkness worsens disorientation.
❌ D — Lorazepam (benzo) can cause paradoxical agitation and worsen confusion in elderly patients.
❌ E/G — Visitors and stimulating media increase overstimulation for a delirious patient.
❌ H — Daytime napping disrupts the sleep-wake cycle and worsens delirium.
SATAWhich actions should the nurse take to help reorient Mrs. Smith? Select all that apply.
✅ Correct: A, B, F
✅ A — Environmental cues (clock, calendar) provide continuous passive reorientation.
✅ B — Verbal reminders of date/time/place directly address disorientation.
✅ F — Always identifying yourself reduces fear and confusion.
❌ C — Minimizing all interactions removes needed supervision and support.
❌ D — Never use pet names — always address the patient by their name.
❌ E — Quizzing the patient is frustrating and not therapeutic; this is NOT reorientation.
Q4 · Single AnswerWhich statement by Mrs. Smith's daughter indicates a need for further teaching about delirium?
✅ Correct: C — This statement needs correction. Delirium is often fully reversible if the underlying cause is treated — even in patients with pre-existing mild cognitive impairment. The daughter's belief that recovery is impossible is a dangerous misconception that could lead to therapeutic nihilism. The nurse must clarify that treating the infection and dehydration can fully resolve the delirium.
⚡
Case 1 Recap: Delirium = acute and reversible. Causes: infection, dehydration, meds, metabolic imbalances. Priority = treat the underlying cause. Avoid benzos and restraints. Reorient gently. Ensure a safe, calm, adequately lit environment.

📋 Case Study 2: Mr. Smith — Alzheimer's Disease

76-year-old male | Mild Alzheimer's, Progressing

Clinical Scenario

Mr. Smith is a 76-year-old retired teacher brought to primary care by his wife. She reports forgetfulness of recent conversations, struggling to make coffee, misplacing objects, and getting lost in his own neighborhood. He has become easily frustrated and withdrawn in social settings. Diagnosis: Mild Alzheimer's, one year ago, progressing. PMH: T2DM, hyperlipidemia. Meds: metformin, atorvastatin, donepezil (Aricept).

Assessment Findings

General: Well-nourished, appropriately dressed but overdressed for the occasion

Neuro: Alert but confused about day of week, cannot recall 3 recent events, difficulty following multi-step instructions

Behavioral: Anxious, looks to wife for reassurance, hesitant to speak

Functional: Basic self-care intact but needs reminders for meds, hygiene, appointments

NCLEX Questions
Q1 · Single AnswerWhich early sign of Alzheimer's disease is Mr. Smith exhibiting?
✅ Correct: B — Misplacing objects and struggling with familiar tasks. Early Alzheimer's presents with memory loss affecting daily life, difficulty with problem-solving, and trouble completing familiar tasks. A — Alzheimer's loses recent memory first, not old. C — Sudden fluctuating confusion describes delirium, not dementia. D — Tremors and movement disorders are characteristic of Parkinson's disease.
SATAWhich nursing interventions would best support Mr. Smith's cognitive function and independence? Select all that apply.
✅ Correct: A, B, D, F
✅ A — Structured routine reduces confusion and anxiety in AD.
✅ B — Physical and mental exercise can help slow cognitive decline.
✅ D — Written cues and memory aids support independence and reduce frustration.
✅ F — Safe Return Program provides identification and safe return if wandering occurs.
❌ C — "Any assistance" ignores safety needs; assist at highest level of functional ability, but not zero assistance.
❌ E — A constantly changing environment worsens confusion; routine and familiarity are therapeutic.
Q3 · Single AnswerMrs. Smith asks if there are medications that can cure Alzheimer's disease. What is the best nurse response?
✅ Correct: B — Alzheimer's disease is irreversible with no known cure. Cholinesterase inhibitors like donepezil can temporarily slow progression and are only marginally effective. They do NOT stop or reverse the disease. Option A gives false hope. Option C is false — irreversible. Option D is inaccurate — treatments do exist.
Q4 · Single AnswerMrs. Smith says, "I feel guilty about thinking of getting help. I promised to care for him myself, but I'm so overwhelmed." What is the best nursing response?
✅ Correct: B — This response acknowledges caregiver burden with empathy and offers concrete, practical resources (respite care, support groups) without dismissing her feelings or removing her caregiving role. A — Prescriptive, dismisses emotional context. C — Harmful and non-therapeutic. D — Deflects and compares, which is not therapeutic communication.
SATAWhich safety measures are most important for Mr. Smith at this stage? Select all that apply.
✅ Correct: A, D, E
✅ A — Removing clutter and area rugs prevents falls in cognitively impaired patients.
✅ D — Difficult-to-open locks prevent wandering and getting lost outside.
✅ E — Night lights prevent nighttime falls during episodes of confusion.
❌ B — Unsupervised walks are unsafe given wandering risk; he already got lost in his own neighborhood.
❌ C — Independent med management is unsafe; he already needs reminders — errors could cause harm.
❌ F — Lorazepam is a benzodiazepine; it worsens confusion and disorientation in dementia patients and does NOT treat memory impairment.

🧠 Clinical Reasoning Patterns — NCLEX Tips

  • Sudden change from baseline in dementia patient = investigate for DELIRIUM first — assess vitals, labs, meds, infection markers
  • Benzodiazepines in elderly with delirium = almost always wrong on NCLEX unless specifically justified for severe alcohol withdrawal
  • Restraints = almost always wrong — worsen agitation and increase fall/injury risk
  • Treat the underlying cause = always the priority in delirium before any pharmacological symptom management
  • Caregiver teaching error questions: Look for statements suggesting irreversibility of delirium, use of inappropriate meds, or unsafe independence

🚨 Must-Know Safety Rules

🚫
Never Do These: Never use Haldol in dementia-related psychosis. Never apply restraints without justification. Never use benzos as first-line for delirium. Never start memantine in mild AD. Never allow wandering patients unsupervised outdoors.
✅
Always Do These: Always approach from the front. Always use patient's name. Always treat underlying cause in delirium. Always establish routine in dementia. Always ask: what unmet need is this behavior expressing?
⭐
Top NCLEX Triggers: 1) Sudden change in dementia patient → assess for delirium. 2) Benzo for elderly confused patient → question/hold. 3) Haldol for dementia psychosis → contraindicated. 4) Restraints for agitation → wrong answer. 5) Memantine for mild AD → wrong stage.
💡
Pseudodementia Rule: When you see memory loss, cognitive changes, withdrawal, and fatigue in an older adult — consider depression (pseudodementia). Treat the depression → cognitive symptoms may resolve.

🩺 Priority Nursing Actions by Scenario

⚡ Acute Confusion in Elderly Patient

Priority order:

  1. Assess vitals, neuro status, pain
  2. Determine baseline mental status (ask family)
  3. Review all medications — any new or high-risk drugs?
  4. Obtain labs (BMP, CBC, UA)
  5. Ensure safe environment (sitter, call light, bed alarm)
  6. Calm, reassuring communication
  7. Treat underlying cause when identified
  8. Hold benzos; avoid restraints; avoid Haldol
🧩 Patient with Known Dementia, Worsening

Priority order:

  1. Rule out superimposed DELIRIUM (sudden change = new cause)
  2. Assess for infection, dehydration, medication issues
  3. Review and adjust medications with provider
  4. Implement safety measures (falls, wandering)
  5. Use validation therapy if agitated by reality correction
  6. Support family/caregiver with resources
  7. Maintain structured daily routine
  8. Promote highest functional ability in ADLs

🌟 One-Liner Summary: The Big Picture

Delirium = acute, reversible, treat the cause NOW or permanent damage occurs. Dementia = chronic, progressive, no cure — protect dignity, preserve function, promote quality of life. Both require a safe environment, calm communication, nonpharmacological interventions first, and careful attention to medications that may harm more than help. The nurse's role is to be the patient's advocate, interpreter, and protector — in their most vulnerable cognitive state.
