Population Health & Community Nursing

Pop. Health Ch.

Community-based health care is a collaborative, person-centered, culturally sensitive model that reaches people where they live, work, socialize, and learn — including the poor and underinsured. Nurses are strategically positioned to improve health care delivery and community health every day.

Key Distinctions

Community Health Nursing

  • Focuses on improving health of individuals, families, and groups within their communities
  • Goals: preserve, protect, promote, and maintain health
  • BSN is recommended entry level (ANA)
  • Considers the individual/family as part of a group at risk

Public Health Nursing

  • Focuses on understanding needs of a population — a collection of individuals with common personal or environmental characteristics
  • Understands: disease trends/patterns, environmental influences, political processes affecting policy
  • Example: using playground injury data to advocate for safer materials

Population Health

  • Covers the continuum from public health prevention through population disease management
  • Includes collaborative partnerships: communities, public health, education, business, local government
  • Goal: improve health equity and the health of all people
Healthy People 2030 Goals

Five Goals

  • Increase life expectancy
  • Improve quality of life
  • Attain health literacy
  • Achieve health equity
  • Eliminate health disparities through improved health care delivery

Three Key Components of Improved Care

  • Assessment: systematic data collection; monitoring population health status (incident rates, pregnancy rates, accident rates)
  • Policy: health professionals develop public policies supporting population health (e.g., prescription monitoring, naloxone access)
  • Access to care: essential community-wide services available and reachable for all; includes insurance coverage, geographic availability, and provider relationships

Medically underserved populations — characteristics & nursing approach

Who is medically underserved?

  • People in poverty / without homes (582,500 people in U.S. on a single night in 2022)
  • Older adults, people with disabilities
  • Immigrants, refugees, migrant workers
  • Individuals in abusive relationships
  • People with substance use disorders
  • People with serious mental illness

Nursing assessment approach

  • Create a comfortable, nonthreatening environment
  • Use professional interpreter when needed
  • Recognize patients may have priorities other than health (financial, legal, housing)
  • Refer appropriately — social worker for financial, proper resource for legal
  • Do NOT provide financial or legal advice yourself
  • Assess home environment: water, plumbing, safe food storage, pests, temperature, crime level
  • Be alert for signs of abuse, neglect, and substance misuse
Community assessment — three components and elements

Structure / Locale

  • Geographic boundaries
  • Emergency services
  • Water and sanitation
  • Housing, transportation, safety
  • Economic status

Population

  • Age and sex distribution
  • Growth trends, density
  • Education level
  • Predominant ethnic and religious groups

Social System

  • Educational system, government
  • Communication system
  • Welfare system
  • Volunteer programs, health system
Global health & its impact on communities
  • WHO (formed 1948, 150 countries): focuses on universal health coverage, health emergencies, and well-being.
  • Global factors affecting communities: equitable vaccine access, air pollution/climate change, infectious diseases, health care equality, medication availability, health care worker shortages, clean water/sanitation.
  • Emerging infectious diseases: Ebola, H1N1, SARS, Zika, COVID-19 — often result from international travel, crowded urban populations, or climate change.
  • Pandemic effects: disrupt schools/businesses, overwhelm health systems, increase mental health problems, cause supply/medication shortages.
  • Nurses in pandemics: caregivers, educators, supply managers, disease prevention leaders, liaisons between patients and the team.
Specific population concerns — immigrants, people experiencing poverty, abuse victims, patients with mental illness
  • Immigrants: may face poverty, language barriers, limited transportation, legal concerns, housing barriers; higher rates of HTN, DM, infectious disease, stress; may use nontraditional healing practices — assess beliefs without judgment.
  • People without homes: lack employment, shelter, healthy nutrition; higher ER use; chronic health problems worsen due to inability to store food or adhere to medications.
  • Abuse victims: interview privately (separate from possible abuser); use therapeutic communication; reinforce abuse is not the victim's fault; nurses are mandatory reporters for adults 65+, children under 18, and dependent individuals.
  • Patients with mental illness: face homelessness, poverty, medication needs, counseling, vocational assistance; navigating the system is difficult — many remain untreated or undertreated in community settings.
📌
Key distinction: community health nursing focuses on improving health of individuals/families within communities. Community-based nursing focuses on direct illness care to individuals in settings like homes and clinics. Both are part of the health care delivery system — they are not the same thing.

Health, Wellness & Illness Prevention

Health & Wellness Ch.

Health is more than the absence of disease — it is personally defined, influenced by culture, values, lifestyle, and life circumstances. A person may be disease-free and still not be healthy, or have a diagnosed disease and still feel healthy.

Health Models

Health Belief Model (HBM)

  • Explains the relationship between a person's beliefs and health behaviors
  • Key elements: perceived susceptibility, perceived seriousness/severity, perceived benefits, perceived barriers, cues to action
  • Cues to action: media campaigns, advice from others, a friend becoming ill
  • Example: patient refuses colonoscopy until a younger friend is diagnosed with colon cancer

Health Promotion Model (HPM)

  • Defines health as a positive, dynamic state — not merely absence of disease
  • Three focus areas: individual characteristics/experiences → behavior-specific cognitions → behavioral outcomes
  • Nurses modify influencing variables to support behavior change
  • Health promotion behaviors improve health, function, and quality of life

Holistic Health & Nursing

  • Emphasizes relationship among body, mind, and spirit
  • Empowers patients to participate in recovery and take responsibility for health
  • Common interventions: meditation, music therapy, reminiscence, relaxation therapy, therapeutic touch, guided imagery
  • Can be combined with conventional medicine
Three Levels of Prevention
LevelGoalExamples
Primary preventionReduce incidence of disease — health promotion before disease occursImmunizations, nutrition programs, physical fitness, occupational safety, environmental sanitation, sex education, hearing protection
Secondary preventionDetect and treat early disease; prevent spread or progression; limit disabilityScreening for DM/HTN/cancer, early treatment of hypertension, colonoscopy, mammogram, identifying new disease cases
Tertiary preventionMinimize effects of permanent or irreversible disease/disability; maximize functionSpinal cord injury rehab, cardiac rehab, managing chronic disease complications, hospice and palliative care
Risk Factors

Nonmodifiable risk factors

  • Cannot be changed
  • Age, sex, genetics, family history
  • Example: older adults have higher risk for heart disease, cancer, Alzheimer's, influenza/pneumonia
  • Nurses use these with assessment data and patient preferences to select appropriate prevention strategies
  • Screen patients for age-appropriate preventive services

Modifiable risk factors

  • Can be changed — often involve behavior
  • Smoking, alcohol use, unhealthy diet, obesity, physical inactivity, poor sleep
  • Poor control of HTN, elevated lipids, elevated glucose, excessive sun exposure
  • Stress (when severe, prolonged, poorly managed) → heart disease, sleep problems, HTN, GI issues
  • Environmental risks: air, water, soil, workplace, neighborhood conditions
Transtheoretical Model of Change (5 Stages) — nursing interventions by stage
StagePatient statementNursing approach
1. Precontemplation"There is nothing I really need to change."Plant seeds of awareness; provide nonjudgmental information; do NOT push change
2. Contemplation"I have a problem I think I need to work on." (next 6 months)Explore ambivalence; reinforce benefits; discuss barriers
3. Preparation"I think I might try again in a few weeks."Help set specific goals; identify resources; build a plan
4. Action"I am really working hard to stop smoking." (up to 6 months)Provide support and encouragement; anticipate relapse triggers; reinforce effort
5. Maintenance"I need to avoid people who smoke." (6+ months)Help prevent relapse; connect to support groups; normalize setbacks as part of learning
📌
Relapse should be framed as part of learning, not failure. Nursing interventions must match the patient's current stage of change — pushing Action-stage strategies on a Precontemplation patient is counterproductive.
Variables influencing health beliefs & practices

Internal variables

  • Developmental stage
  • Intellectual background and education
  • Perception of functioning (subjective: pain, fatigue; objective: BP, lung sounds)
  • Emotional factors (stress, depression, fear)
  • Spiritual factors (values, hope, meaning, treatment decisions)

External variables

  • Family role and practices
  • SDOH (economic stability, education access, neighborhood)
  • Culture (beliefs, customs, views of health care, cause of illness)
  • Social support and peer influence
  • Health care accessibility and insurance status
Illness behavior & impact on patient and family
  • Illness = a state in which physical, emotional, intellectual, social, developmental, or spiritual functioning is impaired — the feeling of poor health. A person may have disease without feeling ill, or feel ill without diagnosable disease.
  • Acute disease: usually reversible, short duration, abrupt onset, intense symptoms that subside relatively quickly.
  • Chronic disease: lasts >6 months, usually irreversible, affects one or more body systems, involves periods of better functioning and relapses. ~60% of U.S. adults have at least one chronic disease; ~40% have two or more.
  • Impact on family: behavioral/emotional changes, altered body image, changed self-concept, family role reversal, changed family dynamics. Nurses observe these changes and plan care to support adjustment.
  • Patients more likely to seek care if symptoms disrupt normal routine or feel serious. Older adults may delay due to mistrust, desire to preserve independence, or underestimating symptoms.
Compassion fatigue — recognize and prevent

What it is

  • Combination of secondary traumatic stress (from close relationships with suffering patients/families) and burnout (from chronic lack of resources)
  • Effects: emotional exhaustion, sleep/appetite changes, reduced empathy, impaired focus, hopelessness, anxiety, irritability, poor judgment, absenteeism, turnover

Prevention strategies

  • During work: journaling, mindfulness, meditation, walking with coworkers, debriefing after difficult experiences
  • Personal: nutritious diet, adequate sleep, regular exercise, work-family balance, allowing time to grieve patient loss, mentorship
  • Many organizations now offer programs with education, coping tools, and resilience building
Patient teaching for behavior change — key principles
  • Practice active listening; build rapport; assess what the patient already knows.
  • Ask about barriers to diet, exercise, and medication adherence.
  • Help set goals aligned with standards (e.g., Physical Activity Guidelines: 150–300 min/week moderate intensity, strength training 2 days/week).
  • Tailor materials to literacy level, culture, and patient needs.
  • Involve family, social groups, and social media support when appropriate.
  • Use teach-back to evaluate understanding — ask the patient to demonstrate or explain what they learned.
Federally Qualified Health Centers (FQHCs)
  • Government-supported centers located in medically underserved communities.
  • Address causes of poor health: nutrition, housing, mental illness, SDOH.
  • Reduce barriers to primary care: cost, lack of insurance, distance, language.
  • Nurses in FQHCs use a holistic approach — an important SDOH resource to include in discharge planning.
⚠️
NCLEX trap: primary prevention = before disease occurs (immunizations, education). Secondary = early detection/screening. Tertiary = minimizing effects of established disease/disability. Know which level applies to each nursing action.

Social Determinants of Health & Health Disparities

Concept 49

Core Definitions

  • Health disparities: differences in health or healthcare closely linked to social, economic, or environmental disadvantage.
  • Health equity: attainment of the highest level of health for all people — social justice in health.
  • SDOH: conditions where people live, work, learn, and socialize that shape health outcomes.
  • Inequality vs inequity: not every inequality is an inequity — inequities are avoidable and unjust.

Five SDOH Domains (HP 2030)

  • Economic stability
  • Education access and quality
  • Health care access and quality
  • Neighborhood / built environment
  • Social and community context

Goal: Health equity is the goal; disparities measure how far from it we are.

Vulnerable Populations

  • Uninsured / underinsured / homeless
  • Migrant workers, immigrants, refugees
  • Racial/ethnic minorities
  • Elderly, veterans, chronically ill
  • People with disabilities (cognitive, sensory, physical)
  • Sexual/gender minorities
  • People with limited English proficiency
  • Victims of violence / trafficking

  • Social support networks

Theoretical Frameworks for Understanding Disparities

Social-Ecological Model

  • Microsystem: immediate environment
  • Mesosystem: interactions between systems
  • Exosystem: outside systems (indirect)
  • Macrosystem: culture, laws, social structures
  • Chronosystem: time/life transitions

Helps nurses identify barriers and intervention points at each level.

Structural-Constructivist Model

  • Race/ethnicity are socially constructed categories
  • Disparities result from social meaning assigned to these categories
  • Changing systems and shared meanings can reduce disparities

Cultural Competence Model

  • Explore and respect patient beliefs and values
  • Build trust; find common ground
  • Recognize personal bias
  • Use interpreter services
  • Criticism: can over-focus on individual providers rather than structural causes
Core Nursing Competencies — Health Disparities (Concept 49)
  • Define populations at increased risk because of SDOH.
  • Assess population data to identify and evaluate disparities.
  • Understand public health systems, laws, and policies.
  • Develop culturally and linguistically responsive interventions.
  • Identify ethical principles protecting vulnerable populations.
  • Collaborate with other professionals to address inequities.
  • Challenge biases and barriers affecting health outcomes.
High-yield SDOH nursing actions for exam questions
  • Screen during intake for transportation, food insecurity, housing instability, and insurance.
  • Use teach-back and low-literacy, culturally responsive materials.
  • Ask what resources are realistically available before teaching diet, medications, or follow-up plans.
  • Document barriers clearly and refer to case management/social work early.
  • Never use family, friends, or support staff as interpreters — always use a certified professional interpreter.
  • Avoid assuming understanding; always verify comprehension with teach-back.
⚠️
NCLEX priority: a discharge instruction is NOT effective if it ignores money, transportation, literacy, follow-up access, language, or community resources. The highest-priority nursing target is avoidable and unacceptable disparities.

Grief, Loss, Bereavement & Suffering

Ch. 36
Types of Loss

By Cause

  • Necessary loss: normal life change expected with maturation (e.g., child starting school)
  • Maturational loss: type of necessary loss linked to normal development across the lifespan
  • Situational loss: sudden, unexpected, not part of normal development (e.g., accident, divorce, job loss)

By Perception

  • Actual loss: person can no longer feel, hear, see, or know the lost person/object (e.g., amputation, death)
  • Perceived loss: experienced internally; may not be obvious to others (e.g., loss of confidence, feeling rejected) — can be just as painful as actual loss
  • Ambiguous loss: uncertainty with no finality (e.g., dementia, missing person)

5 Categories of What Is Lost

  • Loss of possessions or objects
  • Loss of known environment (hospitalization, relocation)
  • Loss of a significant other (divorce, death, caregiver)
  • Loss of an aspect of self (body part, function, identity)
  • Loss of life (one's own or a loved one's)
Grief — Core Concepts

What grief is

  • Individualized response to a loss — real, perceived, or anticipated
  • Emotional, cognitive, physical, social, and spiritual — it is a process, not a state
  • Cannot be prevented; is highly personal; requires a great deal of energy
  • Cyclical rather than linear; memories/anniversaries may reactivate it
  • Most people recover within about 1 year after major loss, but grief is never completely "finished"

Factors affecting grief response

  • Human development / age / developmental stage
  • Nature of loss: sudden vs expected, traumatic, suicide, homicide
  • Quality of the relationship to the deceased
  • Coping strategies (healthy vs unhealthy)
  • Socioeconomic status and available resources
  • Culture, ethnicity, spirituality, religion
  • Prior losses and prior mental health history
  • Social support systems
Types of Grief
TypeDescriptionExam clue / Risk factors
Normal (uncomplicated)Universal emotional, physical, cognitive, behavioral, social & spiritual response to lossSadness, tearfulness, yearning, anger, disbelief — but functioning gradually returns
Anticipatory griefGrieving before the actual loss, with prolonged or expected lossCaring for loved one with dementia/ALS/cancer; 4 phases: accepting inevitability → concern → rehearsing death → imagining future
Disenfranchised griefLoss is not socially recognized or openly supportedEx-spouse, pet, suicide, homicide, terminated pregnancy, health care worker loss, same-sex partner "not out"
Ambiguous lossUncertainty and lack of finality (type of disenfranchised grief)Dementia/brain injury (physically present, psychologically absent); kidnapping, MIA, POW (physically absent, present in mind)

Subtypes of complicated grief

Chronic grief

Normal grief symptoms continue for years or decades — never resolves.

Exaggerated grief

Maladaptive or self-destructive behavior, psychiatric symptoms, obsession. Suicide risk may increase.

Delayed grief

Response is postponed — loss is too overwhelming. A later loss may trigger buried grief.

Masked grief

Person doesn't realize their physical/functional problems (headaches, tachycardia, rashes) come from grief.

Common symptoms of grief — assessment guide

Feelings

  • Sorrow, fear, anger, guilt
  • Anxiety, loneliness, fatigue
  • Helplessness, hopelessness
  • Yearning or relief

Cognitions

  • Disbelief, confusion
  • Memory problems, inability to concentrate
  • Difficulty making decisions
  • Sensing the deceased's presence (normal)

Physical sensations

  • Headaches, nausea, chest tightness
  • Insomnia, shortness of breath
  • Weakness, appetite changes
  • Dry mouth
Children's concepts of death — developmental stages
  • Birth to 2: cannot understand death but react to separation through fussiness, sleep changes, bowel/bladder changes.
  • Ages 3–5: limited understanding; may believe death is reversible ("coming back").
  • Ages 6–9: beginning to understand finality; may show regressive or aggressive behaviors; know it can happen to others.
  • Ages 10–12: understand death is final and universal; anger, guilt, depression may occur.
  • Adolescents: adult-like understanding; may withdraw or act out; assess for suicide risk.
Mourning vs bereavement
  • Mourning: the outward expression of grief; includes behaviors people use to cope; often culturally shaped (e.g., Jewish shivah).
  • Bereavement: the period of sadness following the death of a significant person.
  • Nurses support bereaved individuals by allowing them to talk, reassuring their feelings are normal, and encouraging delay of major decisions when possible.
Hope at end of life
  • Hope is multidimensional — it energizes, comforts, gives meaning, and helps people live fully even near death.
  • Hope does not always mean hope for cure — it may shift to hope for: pain relief, attending an event, seeing a loved one, peace, dignity, or a meaningful death.
  • Supports hope: strong relationships, emotional connectedness, faith, humor, positive memories, attentive caregivers.
  • Diminishes hope: abandonment, isolation, uncontrolled symptoms, being devalued.
🚨
Red flags requiring professional help: enduring hopelessness, suicidal thoughts or statements ("I can't go on"), self-destructive behavior, or inability to move forward are signs of complicated grief. Always assess safety first.

Grief Theories & Models

Grief theories help nurses understand patterns, but grief is individualized and not linear — newer models emphasize cyclical movement, not fixed stages.

Theory / ModelKey Stages or ComponentsExam takeaway
Kübler-Ross Stages of DyingDenial → Anger → Bargaining → Depression → AcceptanceMost recognized model; not meant to be rigid or sequential — people move back and forth
Bowlby's Attachment TheoryNumbing → Yearning & searching → Disorganization & despair → ReorganizationHighlights physical symptoms and emotional distress; eventual adjustment
Worden's Grief Tasks1. Accept reality of loss 2. Experience the pain 3. Adjust to world without deceased 4. Emotionally relocate deceased & move forward"Tasks" — something the person actively does, not passive stages
Rando's "R" ProcessRecognize → React → Recollect & reexperience → Relinquish old attachments → Readjust → ReinvestMore comprehensive than Kübler-Ross; emphasizes letting go and rebuilding
Dual Process ModelOscillation between loss-oriented activities (grieving, dwelling on memories) and restoration-oriented activities (adapting, new roles, routines)Most current model — emphasizes back-and-forth movement; neither orientation is "wrong"
📌
Main takeaway from all grief theories: older theories emphasized stages or tasks; newer theories emphasize individuality, unique meaning-making, and cyclical movement. Nurses should never impose a grief timeline on patients or families.
Nursing diagnoses related to grief (NANDA)
  • Hopelessness (often the priority diagnosis)
  • Dysfunctional Grief / Anticipatory Grief
  • Death Anxiety
  • Impaired Family Coping
  • Spiritual Distress
  • Chronic Pain, Fatigue, Low Nutritional Intake
  • Denial About Illness Severity
Expected outcomes for grieving patients — evaluation guide
  • Talking about the loss without becoming overwhelmed
  • Improved sleep and appetite
  • Improved energy; easier social interaction
  • Better decision-making ability
  • Reorganized daily life; return of humor
  • Renewed relationships; decreased inner pain

Therapeutic Communication & Nurse Role

✓ Helpful nursing responses

  • Listen. Be present — physically and psychologically.
  • Use silence, appropriate therapeutic touch, and active listening.
  • Encourage the person to tell the story: "Tell me more."
  • Normalize grief as individualized and non-linear.
  • Ask about cultural, spiritual, or religious preferences.
  • Encourage support systems; refer early when needed.
  • Provide anticipatory guidance about what to expect.
  • Acknowledge feelings without trying to "fix" them.
  • "This must be very difficult for you."
  • "I'm sorry this is happening."

✗ Avoid (non-therapeutic)

  • "I know how you feel." — assumes understanding
  • "At least they are in a better place." — minimizes loss
  • "Everything happens for a reason." — dismissive cliché
  • "Don't cry." / "You need to stay strong." — shuts down grief
  • "In a year you'll have forgotten." — imposes timeline
  • Withdrawing emotionally while focusing only on physical care
  • Using family/friends as interpreters for non-English speakers

Nurse's role with grieving client/family

  1. Support and help the person grieve.
  2. Encourage the person to accept support.
  3. Assist in discovering meaning from the loss.
  4. Seek professional assistance when indicated.
  5. Incorporate cultural differences into care.

Nurse self-awareness

  • Death anxiety can lead to emotional distancing or avoidance.
  • Healthy coping: reflection, discussion, arts, meditation, self-care.
  • Your discomfort should never stop the patient or family from expressing grief.
  • Personal assumptions and attitudes require ongoing reflection.
Presence — a core therapeutic tool

Presence means being both physically and psychologically with the patient and family — listening and acknowledging the legitimacy of their pain and impending loss. Sometimes the most therapeutic action is simply sitting, saying nothing, and holding a hand.

Life review, reminiscence & storytelling
  • Life review: structured reflection on one's life, often facilitated by another person — helps patients find meaning, perspective, legacy, and connection near the end of life.
  • Reminiscence: informal, spontaneous remembering of life events.
  • Nurses can encourage: journals, scrapbooks, letters, memory books, recorded messages/videos for loved ones.
Helpful open-ended assessment questions (Ch. 36)
  • "What is the most important thing I can do for you right now?"
  • "What do you understand about your diagnosis?"
  • "You seem sad today. Can you tell me more?"
  • "What does this loss mean to you?"
  • "What would be most helpful to you right now?"
Bereavement facilitation — supporting families after death
  • Reinforce that the loss is real; discuss facts of the loss openly.
  • Help survivors solve practical problems.
  • Encourage asking for help and safe social connection.
  • Remind survivors that anniversary reactions are normal.
  • Normalize behaviors like poor concentration or sensing the deceased's voice.
  • Watch for harmful coping such as substance misuse.

Advance Care Planning & Advance Directives

Ch. 8

Advance care planning is an ongoing process — not just a form. The Patient Self-Determination Act (1991) requires healthcare agencies to ask patients about ADs and provide information.

Durable Power of Attorney for Health Care (DPOAHC)

  • Names a person to make healthcare decisions if the patient loses decisional capacity.
  • NOT the same as financial power of attorney.
  • The designated person does not act until the provider determines the patient lacks decision-making capacity.
  • Decision-making capacity requires: ability to receive information, evaluate/process it, and communicate a treatment preference.

Living Will

  • Legal document describing what life-sustaining treatments a person does or does not want if unable to decide later.
  • Common decisions included: CPR, mechanical ventilation, artificial nutrition/hydration.

DNR / DNAR

  • A provider order stating CPR should not be attempted if cardiac or respiratory arrest occurs.
  • By law, CPR must be initiated unless a valid DNR exists.
  • Some states allow portable DNRs; POLST forms may document wishes across settings.
⚠️
CPR at end of life: CPR can be violent, painful, and may prevent a peaceful death. Many patients and families do not realize CPR was not originally designed for patients with end-stage disease — education is critical. The nurse's role is to inform, advocate for documented wishes, and support informed decisions without imposing personal values.
Nurse's role in advance care planning
  • Help patients think through their goals and values before a crisis occurs.
  • Help families understand options and implications.
  • Support informed decisions without influencing them based on personal values.
  • Recognize that advance care planning is a process — not a single conversation or form.
  • Assess for ADs, DPOAHC, living will, and DNR status on admission and at transitions of care.
Common decisions in advance care planning
  • Continuing or stopping curative treatments
  • Transition to hospice or palliative care
  • Location of care (home, hospital, hospice facility)
  • Artificial nutrition and hydration
  • Code status (Full code, DNR, DNI)
  • Desired spiritual or religious support at end of life
  • Preferences for death: who should be present, where, under what conditions

Palliative Care vs Hospice

Concept 53 · High yield

Palliative care developed because it was not ethical to reserve expert symptom relief and whole-person care only for patients who were clearly dying. It is now a core sphere of professional nursing education (AACN Essentials).

🌿 Palliative care

  • Begins at diagnosis of serious illness; continues across the entire illness trajectory.
  • Can occur alongside curative or life-prolonging treatment.
  • Appropriate at ANY age, any diagnosis, any stage, any setting.
  • Addresses physical, emotional, social, cultural, and spiritual needs.
  • Family and caregivers are included as part of the unit of care.
  • May be delivered in person, via telehealth, or hybrid model.
  • Continues into the bereavement period for families after death.

🕊 Hospice care

  • Specific type of palliative care for patients nearing the end of life.
  • Medicare eligibility: serious progressive illness + life expectancy ≤6 months if disease follows expected course.
  • Curative treatment stops; focus shifts entirely to comfort and dignity.
  • Core services: 24/7 nursing/medical availability, symptom management, family support, bereavement follow-up, trained volunteers, respite care.
  • Can be home, hospice center, nursing facility, or hospital.
  • Originally most hospice patients had cancer; now also dementia, COPD, cardiac disease, neurologic disease.
High-yield distinctions
  • ⭐ All hospice is palliative, but not all palliative care is hospice.
  • Palliative care ≠ end-of-life care ≠ giving up — it can start the day of diagnosis.
  • Two major patient fears at EOL: being a burden to family and being in pain.
  • Early palliative referral improves: treatment planning, goal setting, symptom relief, quality of life, coping, and patient outcomes.

Levels of palliative nursing practice

Primary palliative nursing (ALL nurses)

  • Basic pain & symptom management
  • Common communication skills
  • Discussing quality of life & advance directives
  • Recognizing community resources
  • General support for seriously ill patients

Specialty palliative nursing (advanced)

  • Complex/refractory symptom management
  • Advanced communication & conflict resolution
  • Navigating changing care needs across trajectory
  • Culturally sensitive advanced care
Interprofessional palliative care team

Core NCP services: chaplaincy, social work, nursing, medicine, pharmacy.

Extended team may include: psychologist/psychiatrist, case manager, child life, art/music therapy, bereavement counselors, volunteers, Reiki/massage/acupuncture/pet therapy.

Caregiver burden — recognize and address
  • Physical, emotional, social, and financial stress on family/caregivers.
  • Risk increased by: high caregiving demands, low resources, caregiver age, ADL dependence of care recipient, socioeconomic inequities.
  • Nursing actions: assess caregiver stress, teach coping strategies, connect to respite/services, encourage support groups, promote caregiver self-care and help-seeking.
  • CBT, mindfulness, and support groups reduce burden.
End-of-life nursing priorities
  • Promote dignity, comfort, hope, and family involvement.
  • Coordinate interprofessional care.
  • Support goals-of-care conversations and advance directives.
  • Respect person-centered and culturally sensitive care.
  • Comfort-care pearl: IV hydration/tube feeding may increase discomfort in multiorgan shutdown — mouth care and lip moisture often matter more than pushing fluids.
📌
Palliative care also applies in emergencies, pandemics, and mass casualty events — wherever suffering is high and prognosis is uncertain. It is essential in ICUs, EDs, and trauma services, not just oncology.

Comfort Symptom Management at End of Life

Ch. 8

A major nursing goal is comfort. Uncontrolled symptoms increase suffering for both patient and family. The nurse identifies distressing symptoms and advocates for treatment based on patient priorities.

Dyspnea (breathlessness)

Assessment

  • Subjective like pain — only the patient can describe it.
  • Normal SpO₂ does NOT rule out dyspnea.
  • Assess: intensity, triggers (talking, eating, stairs), emotional responses (panic, anxiety, anger, frustration)

Interventions

  • Morphine — first-line even without pain; reduces air hunger, anxiety & O₂ demand
  • Benzodiazepines (lorazepam) for anxiety-related dyspnea
  • Corticosteroids, bronchodilators, diuretics for specific causes
  • Anticholinergics (atropine, hyoscyamine, scopolamine) for secretions
  • Fan directed at face — evidence-based, very effective
  • Elevate HOB; cool wet cloths; guided imagery; deep breathing
⚠️
Death rattle is usually more distressing to family than to the patient. Manage with: side positioning, towel for secretions, anticholinergics. Avoid oropharyngeal suctioning — it agitates and does not help.
Pain management

Causes near EOL

  • Tumor infiltration of organs, nerves, or bone
  • Osteoarthritis, muscle spasms
  • Immobility-related pain
  • Pre-existing chronic pain

Management

  • Continue scheduled opioids — do not withhold because of renal/respiratory changes in EOL patients
  • As swallowing declines → sublingual, rectal, transdermal, IV, or SQ routes
  • Older adults: start low, increase cautiously; pain is often undertreated
  • Renal failure: fentanyl preferred over morphine (metabolite accumulation worsens delirium)
Nausea & vomiting
  • Common in last week of life, especially with advanced HIV/AIDS, breast, stomach, GI, and GYN cancers.
  • Causes: uremia, hypercalcemia, increased ICP, constipation/impaction, bowel obstruction, opioid side effect (may resolve in a few days).
  • Medications: prochlorperazine, ondansetron, dexamethasone, metoclopramide.
  • If constipation is the cause: Fleet enema, mineral oil enema, gentle disimpaction.
  • Environmental: remove odors, comfortable temperature; aromatherapy (peppermint, chamomile, lavender) may help some patients.
Agitation, delirium & terminal restlessness
  • First: assess for reversible causes — pain, urinary retention, constipation, medication effects.
  • Delirium = acute, fluctuating change in mental status (inattention, disorganized thinking, altered LOC). Types: hyperactive, hypoactive, mixed.
  • Terminal delirium occurs in last days/weeks; may be caused by opioids, benzodiazepines, anticholinergics, or steroids — reduce/stop contributing agents when possible.
  • Antipsychotics (low-dose haloperidol) for hallucinations/delusions. Do NOT give more than one antipsychotic at a time.
  • Benzodiazepines are NOT first-line for agitation in older adults — can worsen delirium; paradoxical agitation possible.
  • Nonpharmacologic: soothing music, pet therapy, lavender/chamomile aromatherapy, calm environment.
Constipation
  • Expected and predictable with opioids and many analgesics — prevention is easier than treatment.
  • Bowel movement expected at least every 3 days regardless of intake/activity.
  • Stepwise escalation: senna/Peri-Colace/Doxidan → bisacodyl or milk of magnesia → suppository/enema if needed.
  • Start a stimulant laxative prophylactically at the same time opioids are initiated.
Weakness, fatigue & dysphagia
  • Fatigue: balance energy reserves and expenditure; encourage bedrest to prevent falls; hospital/electric beds helpful.
  • As swallowing declines: limit to soft foods/sips; do not force food/fluid; teach family about aspiration risk.
  • Terminal dehydration does not cause distress and may actually promote comfort through endorphin release — IV fluids may worsen secretions, edema, ascites, and nausea.
  • Comfort: moisten mouth, use swabs, apply lip balm.
  • Reassess medications — discontinue nonessential drugs; avoid crushing sustained-release formulations; IM route avoided (painful and unreliable).
Seizures
  • Less common; may occur with brain tumors, advanced AIDS, or known seizure disorders.
  • When oral antiepileptics can no longer be swallowed: rectal diazepam gel or sublingual lorazepam solution.
Refractory symptoms — palliative sedation
  • Used when symptoms are severe, refractory, and unbearable despite standard treatment.
  • Medications: benzodiazepines (midazolam), barbiturates, or short-acting anesthetics (propofol).
  • Intent: relieve suffering by lowering consciousness — NOT to hasten death.
  • Using appropriate symptom-management protocols at EOL does not hasten death.
General comfort measures (skin, mouth, environment)
  • Skin: keep clean, dry, moisturized; monitor incontinence; frequent repositioning
  • Mouth: oral care every 2–4 hours; lip balm; analgesics to oral lesions if needed; artificial tears for eyes
  • Environment: dim lighting, soothing music, minimal noise/odors, familiar photos/objects, comfortable clothing
  • Complementary therapies: massage, music therapy, therapeutic touch, guided imagery, aromatherapy — all reduce pain and anxiety

Approaching Death — Signs, Pathophysiology & Family Teaching

Ch. 8
Pathophysiology of dying

Death occurs when illness or trauma overwhelms the body's compensatory mechanisms. The final common pathway: poor perfusion → organ failure → dysrhythmia → cardiopulmonary arrest.

Cardiovascular failure

Dysrhythmias, MI, cardiogenic shock → inadequate perfusion → tissue ischemia, anaerobic metabolism, acidosis, hyperkalemia

Respiratory failure

PE, HF, pneumonia, lung disease, increased ICP → respiratory arrest; or cardiac arrest triggers respiratory arrest

Multiorgan dysfunction

Toxic metabolites released → MODS → renal/liver failure → severe hypoxia/acidosis → ventricular fibrillation or asystole

Physical signs — days to hours before death

Last 48–72 hours

  • Increased sleeping / reduced LOC → lethargy → coma
  • No fluids or only sips; no appetite; inability to swallow
  • Little/no urine output; dark, concentrated urine; incontinence
  • Restlessness, confusion, disorientation
  • Periods of apnea; Cheyne-Stokes respirations
  • Coldness and mottling / cyanosis / purple discoloration of extremities
  • Weak, irregular, barely palpable pulse; BP dropping
  • Congestion, secretions → death rattle
  • Decreased muscle tone; sagging jaw
  • Vision and hearing changes

Actual signs of death

  • Cessation of heartbeat and respirations
  • Pupils fixed and dilated
  • No response to verbal or tactile stimuli
  • Eyelids open without blinking
  • Jaw relaxed and slightly open
  • Body color waxen/gray pallor
  • Decreasing body temperature
  • Muscles and sphincters relax; urine or stool may be released

Emotional signs & near-death awareness
  • Withdrawal: patient may pull away from surroundings and relationships — this is normal.
  • Vision-like experiences: patients may speak to people not visible, describe journeys, or wave to deceased relatives. Do NOT automatically label as delirium. Affirm the experience; do not argue or redirect.
  • Letting go: agitation or repetitive tasks may suggest unresolved concerns. Hearing loved ones say "It's okay to go" can bring peace.
  • Saying goodbye: touching, hugging, crying, and expressions of love — "I love you," "Thank you," "I'm sorry," "I'll miss you" — are deeply comforting.
Culture & spirituality at end of life — HOPE assessment
  • H: sources of hope and strength
  • O: organized religion
  • P: personal spirituality and practices
  • E: effects on care and end-of-life decisions

Some cultures prefer family-centered (not direct) communication about prognosis. Some religious practices require specific rituals before and after death. Always ask rather than assume.

Pronouncement of death — nursing process
  1. Note the time respirations ceased.
  2. Identify the patient correctly.
  3. Confirm no response to verbal or tactile stimuli.
  4. Auscultate absent heart sounds.
  5. Palpate absent carotid pulse.
  6. Confirm absence of respirations.
  7. Document time and notifications.
  8. Notify medical examiner if required by law.
  9. Document whether autopsy is planned.

State law and agency policy determine whether nurses may pronounce death independently.

📋
The nurse serves as consultant, collaborator, coach, and guide. Never predict an exact time of death — instead, explain the meaning of observed changes compassionately to prepare the family.

Postmortem Care & After-Death Responsibilities

Ch. 36 & 8

Principles of postmortem care

  • Same dignity and respect as for a living patient.
  • Complete promptly to minimize tissue changes.
  • Follow cultural and religious beliefs.
  • Know and follow agency policies.

Organ & tissue donation

  • Specially trained staff make the donation request — nurses provide support.
  • Clarify that brain death = death, even if machines preserve organ circulation.
  • Follow state law and agency policy.
Autopsy — when required and nurse's role

Autopsy is a surgical examination of the body to determine cause/circumstances of death. May be required by law in: homicide, suicide, accidents, suspicious death, or death within 24 hours of admission.

  • The provider usually requests permission; the nurse supports, answers questions, and explains that the body is not deformed.
  • If autopsy is planned, do not remove lines or tubes until cleared.
Standard postmortem care steps
  1. Confirm provider certified death.
  2. Determine if autopsy is ordered.
  3. Validate organ/tissue donation request status.
  4. Identify patient correctly.
  5. Hand hygiene, gloves, and appropriate PPE.
  6. Elevate head of bed; collect ordered specimens.
  7. Ask if family wants to participate or has special rituals/clothing requests.
  8. Remove tubes/lines unless contraindicated by autopsy/donation.
  9. Cleanse body; groom hair; close eyes; insert dentures if possible.
  10. Straighten body; place pillow under head; place pads under hips/perineum.
  11. Clean the environment; offer family privacy for viewing.
  12. Manage belongings respectfully.
  13. Tag and shroud body according to policy.
  14. Document thoroughly.
  15. Transport body respectfully.
Documentation after death — key charting elements
  • Date and time of death
  • Actions taken; who verified/certified death
  • Notifications made (family, provider, ME, etc.)
  • Organ/tissue donation request status
  • Body preparations performed
  • Lines/tubes left in place (if any) and reason
  • Personal belongings — inventory and disposition
  • ID tag placement; body transfer time and destination
  • Special family requests or circumstances
Religious and cultural EOL practices — examples

Christianity

  • Beliefs vary by denomination
  • Roman Catholics may receive Sacrament of the Sick
  • Beliefs about heaven/hell may influence expectations

Judaism

  • Dying person should not be left alone
  • Body must be respected; burial ASAP
  • Embalming, cremation, and display generally not desired
  • Autopsy usually avoided unless required

Islam

  • Body is sacred; autopsy only if legally required
  • Embalming and cremation forbidden
  • Body may be positioned toward Mecca
  • Religious rituals for washing/wrapping needed

Ethics at End of Life

Ch. 8

Voluntary Active Euthanasia (VAE)

  • Provider directly takes action to intentionally cause the patient's death.
  • NOT supported by most U.S. professional nursing organizations.
  • Not legal in the United States.

Medical Aid in Dying (MAID / PAD)

  • Legal in some countries and in 11 U.S. states.
  • Nurses do NOT administer MAID medication.
  • Nurses must: understand applicable law, provide accurate/objective information, allow open discussion without judgment, not impose personal values.
Key ethical concepts at EOL
Withholding / withdrawing life-sustaining treatment
  • Stopping or not starting interventions that no longer benefit the patient, are futile, burdensome, or harmful.
  • Critical distinction: withdrawing treatment does NOT directly cause death — the underlying illness causes death.
  • This is ethically and legally supported when consistent with documented patient wishes or surrogate decisions.
Voluntary Stopping of Eating and Drinking (VSED)
  • When a competent patient consciously chooses to stop eating and drinking to hasten death.
  • Reasons: severe suffering, desire for control, wish to reduce caregiver burden.
  • Nurse must understand: ethical issues, informed consent requirements, and comfort-focused management of terminal dehydration.
Palliative sedation vs hastening death
  • Palliative sedation is used for refractory, unbearable symptoms — the intent is to relieve suffering, NOT to hasten death.
  • Using appropriate EOL symptom protocols does not hasten death — this is a critical ethical and legal distinction.
  • ANA Code of Ethics: nurses have an ethical obligation to provide comfort; they are not responsible for death when providing appropriate symptom relief.
Dying Person's Bill of Rights — key items
  • To be treated as a living human being until death.
  • To maintain hope; to be cared for by people who maintain hope.
  • To express feelings about death without judgment.
  • To participate in decisions about care.
  • To not die alone; to be free from pain.
  • To hear the truth; to have family supported.
  • To die in peace and dignity; to retain individuality.
  • To engage in religious or spiritual experiences.
  • To have the body respected after death.
⚖️
The ANA Code of Ethics and the Dying Person's Bill of Rights are key standards guiding ethical EOL nursing. Nurses are required to be objective and non-imposing when patients or families face difficult EOL decisions — personal values must not influence the care provided.

Rapid Review — All Chapters

Exam prep

Know this cold

  • All hospice is palliative — NOT vice versa.
  • Grief is individualized, cyclical, and NOT linear.
  • Dyspnea is subjective like pain — SpO₂ does NOT rule it out.
  • Hospice = ≤6 month prognosis + serious progressive illness.
  • Withdrawing treatment ≠ causing death — illness causes death.
  • Palliative sedation intent = relieve suffering, NOT hasten death.
  • DPOAHC ≠ financial POA; activates only when capacity is lost.
  • Terminal dehydration may promote comfort — IV fluids may worsen symptoms.
  • Primary prevention = before disease; secondary = early detection; tertiary = minimize disability.
  • Mandatory reporters: suspected abuse of adults 65+, children under 18, and dependent individuals.

Priority nursing actions

  • Assess patient goals of care — always person-centered.
  • Relieve distressing symptoms (pain, dyspnea, agitation) first.
  • Use therapeutic presence and silence at EOL.
  • Refer early for SDOH barriers, caregiver burden, spiritual needs.
  • Use certified interpreter — never family or staff.
  • Start prophylactic bowel regimen with every opioid order.
  • Do NOT force food/fluid near EOL.
  • Match teaching interventions to the patient's stage of change.
  • Interview suspected abuse victims privately.
  • Screen bereaved clients with depression history for suicidal ideation.

Common exam traps

  • Giving unrealistic discharge teaching ignoring SDOH barriers.
  • Using family as interpreter.
  • Equating palliative care with "giving up" or hospice.
  • Imposing a grief timeline on patients or families.
  • Labeling near-death awareness as delirium and medicating it.
  • Suctioning for death rattle — avoid; it agitates and doesn't help.
  • Giving benzodiazepines as first-line for agitation in older adults.
  • Assuming normal SpO₂ means no dyspnea.
  • Pushing Action-stage strategies on a Precontemplation patient.
  • Confusing community health nursing (individual/family) with public health nursing (population).

Tap-to-Flip Flashcards

Palliative care
Holistic, symptom-focused care for any stage of serious illness — can begin at diagnosis, alongside curative treatment.
Hospice
End-of-life comfort care for serious progressive illness; typically ≤6 month prognosis; curative treatment stops.
Anticipatory grief
Grieving before the actual loss occurs — common with terminal illness, dementia, ALS. Can cause emotional exhaustion.
Disenfranchised grief
A loss not socially recognized or supported — e.g., ex-spouse, pet, suicide, terminated pregnancy, same-sex partner "not out."
Ambiguous loss
Grief with no finality — either person is physically present but psychologically absent (dementia), or absent but psychologically present (missing person).
Complicated grief
Prolonged, severe, disruptive grief lasting >1 year, affecting ~10–20% of grievers. Risk factors: suicide, sudden death, child loss, poor support.
Suffering
Severe distress threatening the person's sense of intactness and personhood — failure to respond to unmet needs intensifies it.
SDOH
Conditions where people live, work, learn, and access care that shape health outcomes — economic stability, education, healthcare access, neighborhood, social context.
Health equity
The attainment of the highest level of health for all people — social justice in health. Health disparities are the measurable gaps showing how far we are from it.
DPOAHC
Durable Power of Attorney for Health Care — names a surrogate decision-maker; activates ONLY when patient loses decision-making capacity. ≠ financial POA.
Primary prevention
Reduces incidence of disease BEFORE it occurs — immunizations, education, nutrition, exercise, occupational safety, environmental sanitation.
Secondary prevention
Early detection and treatment of disease — screenings (mammogram, colonoscopy), identifying new cases, early intervention to prevent complications.
Tertiary prevention
Minimizes effects of established, irreversible disease/disability — rehab, managing chronic disease, hospice and palliative care.
Health Belief Model
Explains behavior via perceived susceptibility, seriousness, benefits, barriers, and cues to action. Patients act when they perceive personal risk AND that action will help.
Transtheoretical Model (Stage 1–3)
Precontemplation ("no problem") → Contemplation (ambivalent, 6 months) → Preparation (planning, small changes). Relapse = part of learning, not failure.
Compassion fatigue
Combination of secondary traumatic stress (from patient/family suffering) + burnout (from chronic resource shortage). Signs: exhaustion, reduced empathy, poor judgment, hopelessness.
Medically underserved population
Groups more likely to develop health problems due to excess health risks, limited access, or dependence on others — e.g., people in poverty, homeless, immigrants, older adults, those with mental illness.
Mandatory reporting — nurse obligation
Nurses are mandatory reporters for suspected abuse of: adults 65+, children under 18, and dependent individuals (e.g., people with disabilities). Interview suspected victims PRIVATELY.
