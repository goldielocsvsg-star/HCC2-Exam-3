📚 Key Concepts — Flip to Review
What is the PRIMARY focus of palliative care?hover/tap
Quality of life — NOT curing disease. It addresses physical, psychological, social, and spiritual suffering.
When does palliative care begin?
At the time of diagnosis! It can run alongside curative treatment at any illness stage.
What is "Total Pain"?
Suffering in 4 domains: Physical, Psychological, Social, Spiritual. Think: PPSS
What is the Palliative Performance Scale (PPS)?
An assessment tool measuring functional status in palliative patients — mobility, activity, self-care, intake, consciousness.
What's the nurse's key role in palliative care?
Advocate, holistic assessor, symptom manager, care coordinator, family supporter, and cultural bridge — nurses spend the MOST time with patients.
Name 3 tools used in palliative assessment
1. Pain scales 2. Palliative Performance Scale (PPS) 3. Edmonton Symptom Assessment System (ESAS)

🔑 CRITICAL

"All hospice care is palliative care, but not all palliative care is hospice."
This is a guaranteed NCLEX concept. Hospice is a SUBSET of palliative care.

💭 Patient Preferences at End of Life
Most common patient wish at end of life?
To die at home, free from pain, with dignity, and without being a burden to family.
Where do most deaths still occur?
In hospitals — despite most patients wishing to die at home. Nurses must assess and advocate for patient preferences.
What must nurses assess at end of life?
Goals of care, treatment preferences (DNR, no tube feeding, etc.), AND preferred location for death.

💧 Artificial Hydration at End of Life — NCLEX TRAP
Is loss of appetite normal at end of life?
YES — it is a natural part of the dying process. Do NOT force fluids or nutrition.
What are complications of artificial hydration at EOL?
Fluid overload · Increased secretions · Nausea · Edema · Respiratory distress — MORE harm than good!
What SHOULD you do instead of forcing fluids?
Oral care · Moisten mouth · Apply lip balm · Small ice chips — comfort measures only.

🌊 Kübler-Ross Stages of Grief
1. Denial
"This can't be real." A protective mechanism that buffers the initial shock. Example: "The test results must be wrong."
2. Anger
"Why me? This isn't fair!" Anger may be directed at God, family, medical staff, or the deceased. Nurses should not take it personally.
3. Bargaining
"If I get better, I'll change my ways." Often involves "what if" or "if only" thinking. May reflect guilt or spiritual negotiation.
4. Depression
Deep sadness, withdrawal, crying. Anticipatory grief is common — patient and family may grieve before death occurs.
5. Acceptance
"I'm ready." Not necessarily happy, but at peace. Not everyone reaches this stage, and that's okay.
⚠️ NCLEX Alert: Stages do NOT occur in order · May repeat · Not everyone experiences all stages · Grief has no timeline
Types of Grief — Flip to Review
Anticipatory Grief
Grief that occurs BEFORE the death, common with terminal illness. Patient and family grieve the expected loss.
Disenfranchised Grief
Loss that is not socially recognized. Examples: miscarriage, death of ex-spouse, pet loss, suicide loss.
Complicated Grief
Abnormal grief — persists long-term, disrupts daily functioning, may involve severe depression. Requires professional support.
What to SAY to grieving families
"Tell me how you're feeling." · "I'm here with you." · "Would you like to tell me about them?" · Allow silence.
What NOT to say
❌ "Everything happens for a reason" ❌ "You'll get over it" ❌ "They're in a better place" ❌ "At least they lived a long life"
Physical signs of grief
Fatigue · Sleep disturbances · Appetite changes · Physical chest pain · Sighing · Crying · Muscle weakness

⚖️ Legal Documents — Flip to Review
Living Will
Written document specifying treatments wanted OR refused when patient cannot communicate. Covers: ventilation, artificial nutrition, CPR.
Durable Power of Attorney for Healthcare
Designates a trusted person (healthcare proxy) to make medical decisions when patient is incapacitated.
Dying Patient's Bill of Rights — 3 key ones
Right to: be treated as a living human until death · participate in care decisions · die with dignity and free from pain · not die alone.
When should advance directives be completed?
EARLY — ideally before health crises. While patient is cognitively intact and can express their own wishes. Nurses must advocate for this.

🌸 Postmortem Care — Nursing Responsibilities
First postmortem nursing actions?
Confirm death per policy · Provide privacy for family · Remove tubes/lines if allowed · Clean the body.
Postmortem positioning?
Close eyes and mouth · Position supine with head slightly elevated · Apply ID tags · Allow family time with the body.
Cultural considerations postmortem
Some cultures: require immediate burial · ritual washing by family · family participation in body preparation. Always ask and honor.
