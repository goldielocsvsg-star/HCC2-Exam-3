Skin Anatomy & Physiology

The integument is the body's first and largest line of defense. Understanding its layers explains why tissue integrity breaks down the way it does.

📋 Wound Assessment Basics — What to Assess Every Time

Tissue Integrity Defined

Tissue integrity means structurally intact and physiologically functioning epithelial tissues — including skin, subcutaneous tissue, and mucous membranes. When any layer is disrupted, the nurse's job is to identify the cause, classify the wound, and choose interventions that match the wound's healing trajectory.

Acute vs. Chronic Wound

An acute wound follows the normal healing phases in a predictable timeline (surgical incision, trauma). A chronic wound stalls in the inflammatory phase and fails to progress — often due to ischemia, infection, poor nutrition, or unrelieved pressure.

Skin Assessment Components

  • Temperature: Warmth = inflammation or infection; coolness = ischemia or reduced perfusion
  • Color: Erythema, pallor, cyanosis, or jaundice — skin reflects systemic health status
  • Texture: Edema, induration, bogginess, firmness

Every Wound Assessment Includes

  • Location — mapped to anatomical landmark
  • Size — length × width × depth in cm
  • Tunneling/undermining — probe gently, document clock position
  • Color of wound bed — Red (granulating, healing), Yellow (slough, infected), Black (necrotic/eschar)
  • Cleanliness / odor — purulent odor = infection until proven otherwise
  • Drains present? — type, output, color
  • Exudate type: Serous (clear), Serosanguineous (blood-tinged), Sanguineous (bloody), Purulent (pus = infection)
💡
Only pressure injuries are staged. Do not apply staging terminology to venous ulcers, arterial ulcers, diabetic ulcers, or surgical wounds — this is a major exam distinction.
🧼 Healthy Skin Care & Prevention of Breakdown

Daily Skin Care Principles

  • Use pH-neutral soap — harsh soaps disrupt the skin's acid mantle (pH 4.5–5.5), which is a critical antibacterial defense. Alkaline soaps (pH 9–10) raise skin pH, promoting bacterial colonization
  • Avoid overly long or hot baths — strips sebum and worsens dryness, especially in elderly patients
  • Do not rub vigorously — pat dry to avoid friction-induced skin tears in fragile skin
  • Moisturize promptly after bathing (within 3 minutes) — while skin is still slightly damp to lock in moisture
  • Petroleum-based moisturizers (petrolatum/Vaseline, Aquaphor) are the best-evidence option for dry, cracked skin — they form an occlusive barrier that reduces transepidermal water loss without adding active ingredients that can sensitize fragile skin
  • Avoid Neosporin for simple dry or cracked skin — it is a topical antibiotic and is inappropriate for non-infected dryness. Neomycin (a component of Neosporin) is one of the most common causes of allergic contact dermatitis; reserve antibiotic ointments for actual wound infection as ordered

High-Risk Medication Awareness

  • Corticosteroids: Thin skin, impair collagen synthesis, suppress inflammation and healing — every wound is slower and more fragile
  • Anticoagulants (warfarin, heparin, DOACs): Bruising, hematoma formation, prolonged bleeding after skin tears
  • NSAIDs: Impair platelet function (hemostasis phase) and suppress prostaglandins needed for tissue repair
  • Immunosuppressants: ↓ Neutrophil function = infection risk; ↓ fibroblast activity = stalled healing
⚠️
Skin reflects overall health. Changes in skin color, temperature, or integrity can be the first visible sign of systemic deterioration — vascular insufficiency, infection, malnutrition, or medication toxicity.
🧬 Structural Layers

Epidermis

The outermost avascular layer composed primarily of keratinocytes. Because it has no direct blood supply, it relies entirely on diffusion from the dermis for nutrients. This is why superficial burns and superficial partial-thickness wounds can heal without grafting—the dermis is still intact to support re-epithelialization from the edges and hair follicles inward.

Dermis

A vascular, nerve-rich layer containing collagen and elastin fibers, sweat glands, sebaceous glands, and hair follicles. Collagen gives skin tensile strength; elastin allows it to recoil. When the dermis is damaged, structural integrity is lost and the wound cannot spontaneously regenerate without scarring.

WHY this matters clinically: A Stage 2 pressure injury is painful because the dermis—with its exposed nerve endings—is involved. A full-thickness burn is painless because the entire dermis is destroyed, along with all nerve fibers.

Subcutaneous Tissue (Hypodermis)

Adipose and connective tissue that provides insulation, cushioning, and energy storage. Stage 3 pressure injuries extend into this layer. Because it is poorly vascularized relative to muscle, ischemia can cause extensive subcutaneous necrosis that is not visible from the surface.

Muscle & Bone

Stage 4 pressure injuries extend here. Exposed bone carries an immediate risk of osteomyelitis because bone is highly susceptible to infection once protective tissue is gone.

⚡ Physiologic Functions & Clinical Relevance
  • Protection: Physical, chemical, and microbial barrier. Loss of this barrier in burns or wounds creates an immediate portal of entry for pathogens—this is why infection prevention is the top post-acute priority.
  • Thermoregulation: Eccrine sweat glands cool the body; dermal blood vessels dilate or constrict. Burn patients lose this function completely in affected areas, making temperature instability a systemic complication requiring environmental control.
  • Sensation: Meissner's and Pacinian corpuscles detect pressure, touch, vibration. Loss of sensation (neuropathy, spinal cord injury) removes the body's protective reflex to reposition—the core reason diabetic patients develop foot ulcers silently.
  • Vitamin D synthesis: UV exposure converts 7-dehydrocholesterol to vitamin D3. Hospitalized wound patients with poor nutrition and no sun exposure are frequently vitamin D deficient, which impairs wound healing and immune function.
  • Fluid regulation: The skin prevents transdermal water loss. Large burns cause massive insensible fluid loss, which is factored into fluid resuscitation calculations above and beyond the standard Parkland formula.
👴 Age-Related Changes — Why Older Adults Are Highest Risk

Structural Changes With Aging

  • Epidermal thinning: Flattening of the dermal-epidermal junction reduces adhesion between layers, making the skin far more vulnerable to shear forces that cause tears.
  • ↓ Collagen production: Dermis becomes less resilient; wrinkles form and the skin tears or bruises with minimal force.
  • ↓ Sebaceous activity: Less natural oil = chronic dryness, scaling, and pruritus—elderly patients scratch and damage fragile skin.
  • Prolonged healing: Decreased fibroblast activity, reduced growth factor signaling, and impaired angiogenesis all slow every phase of wound healing.
  • Delayed inflammatory response: The early inflammatory phase of healing (which brings neutrophils to debride the wound) is sluggish in the elderly, meaning infection can establish before defenses mount.

High-Risk Medications in Elderly Patients

  • Corticosteroids: Suppress collagen synthesis and inflammatory response, thin the epidermis, impair immune function → wound healing slows and infection risk rises dramatically.
  • Anticoagulants (warfarin, heparin, DOACs): Minor trauma causes extensive subcutaneous hemorrhage and skin tears. Reduce adhesive use and handle skin with extreme care.
  • NSAIDs: Suppress prostaglandin-mediated early inflammation, which paradoxically impairs the very process that initiates healing.
  • Immunosuppressants: Post-transplant medications devastate wound healing capacity and infection resistance.
🔑
Clinical Reasoning: When an elderly patient on prednisone develops a new wound, your expected healing timeline extends significantly. Set realistic family expectations, increase reassessment frequency, and escalate earlier than you would for a healthy adult.
🩺 Risk Factors for Skin Breakdown

Intrinsic (Patient-Related)

  • Immobility: Cannot relieve pressure by repositioning. Even normal lying/sitting creates ischemic pressure over bony prominences within minutes.
  • Malnutrition: Protein (albumin, prealbumin) is required to build collagen and repair tissue. Vitamin C is essential for collagen cross-linking. Zinc is required for cell division and protein synthesis. Without these, even small wounds fail to heal.
  • Impaired perfusion: Peripheral arterial disease, heart failure, diabetes → tissues don't receive adequate O₂ to support cellular repair. You can have the perfect wound dressing and still fail to heal an ischemic wound.
  • Neuropathy: Removes protective pain sensation. Patient doesn't reposition because they don't feel the pressure building. Classic mechanism in diabetic foot ulcers.
  • Incontinence: Urine contains ammonia and urease-producing bacteria; stool contains digestive enzymes and bacteria. Both cause chemical and infectious maceration of skin.
  • Altered cognition: Cannot participate in repositioning or report pain.

Extrinsic (Environmental)

  • Moisture: Prolonged moisture softens (macerates) the epidermis, making it vulnerable to pressure and friction damage.
  • Shear: Occurs when tissue layers slide in opposite directions—e.g., patient slides down in bed while skin sticks to sheets. This kinks and tears capillaries, causing ischemia even without direct downward pressure.
  • Friction: Dragging a patient across sheets removes the top epidermal layer like sandpaper.
  • Medical devices: Oxygen tubing, nasogastric tubes, casts, and restraints create sustained pressure in areas not typically assessed.
🌊 Peripheral Vascular Disease — Tissue Integrity Implications

Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)

Atherosclerosis narrows arteries → reduced blood flow distal to obstruction. The tissues are chronically oxygen-deprived. Wounds develop because ischemic tissue cannot withstand even minimal pressure or trauma, and they fail to heal because repair requires oxygen-dependent cellular processes.

WHY: ATP synthesis, collagen production, neutrophil killing of bacteria, and angiogenesis are all oxygen-dependent. An ABI (Ankle-Brachial Index) <0.5 means severely compromised perfusion—applying compression here would further reduce the already-inadequate flow and risk wet gangrene.
  • Wounds: deep, punched-out edges, pale/necrotic base, lateral foot/toes
  • Skin: pale, hairless, shiny, cool, thin; thick dystrophic nails
  • Pain: worse with elevation and walking (claudication), better hanging feet dependent
  • Pulses: diminished or absent dorsalis pedis/posterior tibial

Chronic Venous Insufficiency (CVI)

Incompetent venous valves fail to return blood from legs → venous hypertension → capillary congestion → interstitial fluid accumulation → tissue hypoxia and breakdown. The high venous pressure causes fibrin cuffs around capillaries, further impairing O₂ diffusion to tissue.

WHY hemosiderin staining occurs: Red blood cells leak from congested capillaries into the interstitium. Macrophages phagocytose the RBCs and release iron/hemosiderin, which deposits in the skin → permanent brownish discoloration of the gaiter area.
  • Wounds: shallow, irregular, medial malleolus, weeping/moist, often painless
  • Skin: hemosiderin staining, varicosities, lipodermatosclerosis, pitting edema
  • Treatment: compression (first verify ABI ≥0.6) + elevation
🛁 Healthy Skin Maintenance — Patient Teaching With Rationale

Bathing

  • Limit duration/temperature: Hot water strips the stratum corneum's natural lipid barrier, accelerating dryness. Lukewarm water is less desiccating.
  • pH-neutral soap only: The skin surface is normally acidic (pH ~5.5), which inhibits bacterial growth. Alkaline soaps disrupt this acid mantle, increasing infection susceptibility and causing dryness.
  • Elderly: every other day bathing: Daily bathing removes natural sebum and flora without infection benefit in low-activity patients.
  • Pat dry—never rub: Friction from vigorous drying removes fragile surface skin, especially in anticoagulated or steroid-using patients.

Moisturizing

  • Apply within 3 minutes of drying: This window locks in residual moisture. After this point, the skin begins transepidermal water loss and moisturizer is less effective at restoring hydration.
  • Product choice: Occlusive agents (petroleum jelly, mineral oil) form a physical barrier against moisture loss. Humectants (glycerin, urea) draw water into the skin. Emollients (shea butter, jojoba) smooth and soften. CWON recommends Eucerin or petroleum jelly for wound-adjacent skin.
  • Never apply to open wounds unless specifically ordered—occlusive moisturizers in a wound trap bacteria.
💡
Ambulatory patients = lower pressure injury risk. Patients who can walk and move independently shift their weight automatically, relieving capillary pressure before ischemia develops. Pressure injuries become likely when mobility is lost — bedridden, chair-bound, post-operative, or critically ill patients cannot self-reposition and depend entirely on nursing intervention to prevent sustained compression above capillary closing pressure (32 mmHg).

Braden Scale — Why These 6 Domains

  • Moisture: Is skin exposed to prolonged dampness?
  • Activity: How much does patient move around?
  • Mobility: Can patient change/control body position?
  • Nutrition: Adequate protein/calories for repair?
  • Friction & Shear: Is movement creating surface damage?

Pressure Injuries

Pathophysiology, Staging & Management

Pressure injuries are largely preventable. Understanding the exact mechanism of injury dictates every prevention and treatment decision.

⚙️ Mechanism of Injury — The Core Pathophysiology

Pressure injuries form when external mechanical pressure exceeds the capillary closing pressure (approximately 32 mmHg) sustained over a bony prominence. When capillaries are compressed between bone and surface, blood flow stops → tissues become ischemic → cell death begins within hours.

WHY the "a little pressure for a long time OR a lot of pressure for a short time" rule: Ischemic damage is cumulative. The relationship between pressure magnitude and duration is inverse—lower pressures require longer time to cause injury, while high pressures (such as a patient positioned incorrectly on a monitoring device) can cause deep injury within minutes. This is the physiologic basis for q2h repositioning—it interrupts the ischemia before it becomes irreversible cell death.

Pressure → Ischemia → Necrosis Cascade

  • Capillary pressure exceeds → blood flow stops
  • Oxygen and glucose deprivation → anaerobic metabolism begins
  • Lactic acid accumulates → local acidosis
  • Cellular swelling → membrane integrity lost
  • Inflammatory mediators released → pain, edema, redness
  • Prolonged = irreversible cell death → necrosis

Shear & Friction Compound Pressure

  • Shear: When a patient slides down in bed, the skin sticks to the sheets while deep tissue moves downward. This creates oblique tension on perforating vessels, stretching and tearing them → ischemia WITHOUT direct downward pressure over bone. This is why head elevation >30° for extended periods increases sacral pressure injury risk.
  • Friction: Direct mechanical removal of superficial skin layers, lowering the threshold for pressure-induced injury in the same area.
🎯 Complete Staging System — Every Stage With Clinical WHY
Blanchable Erythema

Redness TURNS PALE under fingertip pressure. This is reactive hyperemia—normal physiology where capillaries dilate upon pressure relief to restore perfusion. NOT a pressure injury. Resolves in ½–¾ the duration of pressure exposure. No tissue damage has occurred.

Stage 1

Non-blanchable erythema. Skin intact. Does NOT turn pale—capillary bed has been damaged and can no longer respond. Tissue damage has begun but is reversible if pressure removed immediately. Can be painful, itchy, or have altered temperature/firmness.

Stage 2

Partial thickness loss of dermis. Presents as shallow, open, red-pink wound OR intact/ruptured serum-filled blister. Very painful—nerve endings are exposed. No slough or eschar. May be confused with skin tears, tape burns, IAD—do not stage those as Stage 2.

Stage 3

Full thickness tissue loss through subcutaneous fat. Deep crater with or without undermining/tunneling. Slough may be visible but bone, tendon, and muscle are NOT exposed. Depth varies by anatomical location—nasal bridge Stage 3 is superficial; sacral Stage 3 may be very deep.

Stage 4

Full thickness with exposed bone, tendon, or muscle. Slough and eschar often present. Extensive tunneling/undermining common. High osteomyelitis risk. Healing is extremely prolonged and may require surgical intervention. May lead to systemic sepsis.

Unstageable

Wound base obscured by eschar or slough—cannot determine true depth. Do NOT guess a stage. Stable heel eschar (firm, dry, adherent) = keep it. Unstable/infected eschar anywhere = debride. Stage only after wound base is visible.

Deep Tissue Injury

"Iceberg" injury. Purple/maroon intact skin or blood-filled blister over bony prominence. The surface may look like a bruise but represents deep muscle-bone interface ischemia. May evolve rapidly to expose deeper tissue within days. Mushy/boggy feel on palpation is the key physical sign.

🚨
CRITICAL Staging Rule — Never Downstage: Once a wound is assigned a stage (e.g., Stage 4), it remains that stage as it heals. You document it as "Healing Stage 4"—never retrograde to Stage 3, 2, or 1. Why? Because the tissue that fills in is scar tissue, not fully regenerated dermis—the structural integrity that was present at the lower stage never fully returns.
🛡️ Prevention — The Nursing Priority

Repositioning

  • Bedbound: reposition minimum q2h, more frequently if high Braden risk
  • Chair-bound: reposition/shift weight q1h
  • Use 30° lateral tilt rather than 90° side-lying—90° places full weight on the greater trochanter, a thin-tissue area. 30° distributes pressure across the broader flank.
  • Float heels completely off the mattress—heels have minimal subcutaneous padding. Place a pillow under the calf lengthwise.
  • Use lift sheets and slide boards—dragging creates shear at the sacrum with every transfer.

Surface Selection

  • Standard foam mattress: Not adequate for Braden ≤17 patients
  • Alternating-pressure mattress: Cyclically inflates/deflates air cells to redistribute pressure, mimicking repositioning
  • Low-air-loss mattress: Reduces moisture accumulation; indicated for Stage 3–4 and patients with diaphoresis or incontinence
  • Heel protectors: Offload heels entirely; not substitutes for repositioning

Moisture Management

  • Moisture barrier cream after every incontinent episode
  • Structured incontinence program (scheduled toileting q2–4h)
  • Never leave patient lying in wet/soiled linens
  • pH-neutral skin cleansers—not soap, which disrupts acid mantle

📊 Improvement vs. Deterioration — Evaluating Your Treatment Plan

✓ Signs of Improvement

  • Wound bed progresses: black (necrotic) → yellow (sloughing) → red (granulating) → pink (epithelializing)
  • Measurable reduction in length × width × depth over 2-week intervals
  • Wound edges migrate inward (epithelialization from borders)
  • Exudate decreases in amount and transitions from purulent → serosanguineous → serous
  • Odor diminishes or resolves
  • Periwound skin stops macerating or breaking down
  • Pain level at dressing changes decreasing
  • Labs improving: albumin/prealbumin rising with nutritional support

! Notify Provider Immediately

  • Wound increasing in size or depth despite appropriate treatment
  • New tunneling or undermining developing
  • Drainage changing to purulent (creamy yellow/green) with foul odor
  • Surrounding cellulitis: spreading erythema with warmth, edema, induration
  • Systemic signs: fever, leukocytosis, tachycardia, altered mental status
  • Wound bed turning black (new necrosis) during treatment
  • Bone becoming visible in previously Stage 3 wound → restage to 4
  • Foul odor developing in a previously odor-free wound
Wound Healing

Physiology, Dressing Selection & Complications

Every dressing choice should be a deliberate clinical decision based on moisture balance, wound depth, exudate type, and perfusion status.

🔄 Phases of Wound Healing — What's Actually Happening

Phase 1: Hemostasis (minutes–hours)

Platelet aggregation and clot formation. Fibrin clot acts as scaffold for incoming cells and releases growth factors (PDGF, TGF-β) that recruit healing cells. Why this matters: anticoagulated patients have impaired hemostasis → prolonged bleeding → delayed initiation of repair.

Phase 2: Inflammation (days 1–4)

Neutrophils arrive first to debride bacteria and debris. Macrophages follow to coordinate the entire healing process. Why this matters: steroids, NSAIDs, and immunosuppression blunt this phase → infection risk rises and the cascade for repair never fully launches.

Phase 3: Proliferation (days 4–21)

Fibroblasts synthesize collagen; angiogenesis forms new capillaries (granulation tissue = healthy red, beefy tissue). Wound contracts as myofibroblasts pull edges inward. Why this matters: protein/vitamin C deficiency directly impairs collagen synthesis—without adequate nutrition, this phase stalls.

Phase 4: Remodeling (months–years)

Type III collagen replaced by stronger Type I. Scar matures. Why this matters: healed wound tissue is only ever 80% as strong as original skin—this is why previously healed wounds are at higher risk for recurrence with less provocation.

💧 Moist Wound Healing — The Physiologic Rationale

The gold standard in modern wound care. A moist environment:

  • Allows epithelial cells to migrate across the wound surface without drying out and dying — cells move 2–3× faster in moist environments
  • Enables autolytic debridement — the body's own enzymes (macrophages, proteases) dissolve necrotic tissue when moisture is maintained
  • Reduces pain by preventing nerve ending desiccation
  • Prevents eschar formation, which acts as a physical barrier to healing
  • Maintains growth factor concentrations in wound fluid rather than letting them evaporate
⚠️
Critical Exception — Keep DRY: Poorly perfused wounds (arterial insufficiency, ischemic heel eschar). Applying moisture to an ischemic wound creates a warm, moist environment ideal for bacterial growth without the perfusion needed to fight that infection. Wet eschar on an ischemic heel → wet gangrene. The rule is: moisture balance needs both the moisture AND the blood supply to deliver cells and oxygen.
💡
The Balance Rule: If wound is DRY → add moisture. If wound is WET → absorb moisture. Every dressing choice is about correcting an imbalance, not just covering the wound.

🩹 Hydrocolloid & Calcium Alginate — Deep Dive

These two dressings are the most commonly tested on exams and the most commonly confused in practice. The decision comes down to one question: Is this wound dry or wet?

Hydrocolloid Dressing

What it is: A wafer-style dressing with a hydrophilic inner layer (contains gelatin, pectin, carboxymethylcellulose) bonded to a waterproof, bacteria-proof outer film. When wound fluid contacts the inner layer, it forms a soft gel that keeps the wound moist without adding water or saturating the bed.

✅ USE WHEN:

  • Partial-thickness wounds — Stage 2 pressure injuries, superficial abrasions, skin tears (once aligned)
  • Low to moderate exudate — wound produces some drainage but not enough to leak through the wafer within 24 hours
  • Intact periwound skin — the adhesive border needs healthy skin to seal properly
  • Wounds needing autolytic debridement — the occlusive seal keeps the wound's own enzymes concentrated over the necrotic tissue to break it down naturally
  • Stage 2 pressure injuries — classic indication; protects the shallow wound bed and promotes re-epithelialization
  • Areas subject to friction (sacrum, elbows, heels) where you want durable protection without adhesive trauma on change

❌ DO NOT USE WHEN:

  • Heavy exudate — the wafer will become saturated, leak, and the gel may macerate periwound skin
  • Infected wounds — occlusion in an infected wound creates an abscess; bacteria cannot be cleared without airflow or drainage
  • Full-thickness Stage 3 or 4 wounds — too shallow a dressing for deep cavities; cannot fill dead space
  • Tunneled wounds — does not reach into tunnels to absorb or fill
  • Ischemic/poorly perfused wounds — moist environment without perfusion = bacterial incubator
  • Fragile, very friable skin where adhesive removal causes tearing
💡
Change frequency: Every 3–7 days, or when leaking, lifting, or visibly saturated. The yellow-brown gel under the dressing is NORMAL — it's the hydrocolloid reacting with wound fluid. Do not confuse it with purulent drainage.

Calcium Alginate Dressing

What it is: A highly absorbent fiber dressing derived from seaweed (brown algae). Available as flat sheets or rope/ribbon for packing cavities. When wound exudate contacts the alginate fibers, calcium ions exchange with sodium ions, converting the fibers into a soft, cohesive hydrophilic gel. This gel absorbs fluid, maintains moisture contact with the wound bed, and can be removed in one piece.

✅ USE WHEN:

  • Moderate to heavy exudate — the primary indication; alginate can absorb up to 15–20× its weight in fluid
  • Full-thickness wounds (Stage 3, Stage 4) — available as rope/ribbon for packing deep cavities and tunnels
  • Tunneled or undermined wounds — loosely pack the tunnel with alginate rope to absorb and fill dead space
  • Bleeding or oozing wounds — calcium ions in the alginate activate the clotting cascade, providing mild hemostasis
  • Infected wounds with heavy drainage — absorbs the exudate (which contains bacteria, enzymes, and dead cells) and removes it when the dressing is changed
  • Donor sites after skin grafting — high exudate, needs hemostasis

❌ DO NOT USE WHEN:

  • Dry wounds with minimal exudate — without fluid to activate the gel conversion, alginate will stick to the wound bed and cause pain and trauma on removal; use hydrogel instead
  • Dry necrotic eschar — alginate cannot soften or debride dry eschar without adequate moisture to activate it
  • Third-degree burns — contraindicated per most guidelines; use silver-containing wound products instead
  • Wounds with tunnels of unknown depth — until fully probed and measured; risk of retained dressing material
⚠️
Change frequency: Daily or when saturated (strike-through on outer secondary dressing). Alginate is always paired with a secondary outer dressing (dry gauze or foam) since it is not occlusive. Before removal, irrigate with NS if the gel has dried — prevents trauma to granulation tissue.
🚨
Packing rules: When packing a cavity with alginate rope — fill LOOSELY, never pack tightly. Over-packing compresses granulation tissue buds and kills them. Leave space for drainage and tissue growth.

🩺 Exudate Assessment — What Drainage Tells You
SEROUS

Clear, watery, pale yellow. Normal during early healing—composed of plasma filtered through intact capillaries. Expected in surgical drains post-op. Small amount = normal; large amount = reassess wound.

SEROSANGUINEOUS

Pink-tinged; mix of serous fluid and small amount of blood. Expected in early post-operative wounds (days 1–3). Normal physiologic response as capillaries repair. Reassess if amount suddenly increases (could indicate hematoma).

SANGUINEOUS

Bright red blood. Active bleeding. May indicate wound trauma, coagulopathy, or disrupted vessel. Unexpected sanguineous drainage from a healing wound = notify provider immediately, apply direct pressure.

PURULENT

Creamy, opaque, yellow-green. Represents neutrophils, bacteria, and cellular debris. Always indicates infection. Obtain wound culture BEFORE antibiotics. Foul odor accompanies anaerobic infections.

⚠️ Surgical Wound Complications

Dehiscence

Partial or complete separation of approximated wound edges. Most common 5–8 days post-op when collagen synthesis is peak but not yet mature.

Risk Factors (The WHY):

  • Malnutrition: Inadequate collagen synthesis from protein/vitamin C deficiency
  • Obesity: Poor vascularity of adipose tissue; excessive wound tension
  • Infection: Bacterial collagenase and proteases degrade new collagen
  • Steroids: Suppress fibroblast activity and collagen production
  • Excessive coughing/retching: Raises intra-abdominal pressure, stressing sutures

Nursing Response:

  • Cover wound with sterile saline-moistened gauze immediately
  • Place patient in low Fowler's with knees slightly flexed to reduce tension
  • Notify surgeon STAT
  • Keep NPO; prepare for possible OR return
  • Support emotionally — alarming experience for patient

Evisceration — EMERGENCY

Protrusion of abdominal viscera through wound. Represents complete failure of wound integrity. Potential for bowel strangulation and rapid hemodynamic deterioration.

🚨
FIRST ACTION: Cover with LARGE sterile gauze soaked in normal saline. Keeping organs moist and protected is the immediate priority — desiccation causes rapid cell death and peritonitis.

Sequential Actions:

  • Cover immediately with wet sterile gauze
  • Do NOT push organs back in — risk of contamination and perforation
  • Supine position, knees flexed
  • Call surgeon STAT — this is a surgical emergency
  • Establish IV access; vital signs; O₂; NPO immediately
  • Calm, clear communication with patient: "We're taking care of you, the team is coming"

Wound Drains

  • Assess volume and character q shift minimum
  • Sudden unexpected ↓ in output with suction working + connections intact → clogged tube → notify provider
  • If evacuator cannot maintain vacuum independently → notify surgeon for secondary vacuum system order

🔥 Why Diabetic Neuropathy Causes Ulcers — The Mechanism

Chronic hyperglycemia causes glycosylation of myelin sheaths (the insulating layer around nerve fibers) → segmental demyelination → impaired nerve conduction velocity → sensory neuropathy. The patient loses the ability to detect pressure, temperature, and pain.

Think of it this way: a normal person shifts their weight hundreds of times per day unconsciously in response to pressure signals. A patient with diabetic neuropathy has no such signals. They may walk for hours on a blister or foreign body in their shoe without awareness.

Additionally, autonomic neuropathy reduces sweating → chronically dry, cracked skin → portal of entry for bacteria. Motor neuropathy causes intrinsic foot muscle weakness → foot deformities (hammertoe, claw toe) → abnormal pressure distribution → callus formation at new pressure points → ulceration beneath callus.

🔑
Why glycemic control matters for wound healing: Hyperglycemia (>200 mg/dL) impairs neutrophil chemotaxis and phagocytosis (immune cells can't navigate or kill effectively), reduces fibroblast proliferation, impairs collagen synthesis, and promotes bacterial growth. A wound that would heal in 2 weeks in a euglycemic patient may take months or never heal in a poorly controlled diabetic.

Wound Infection, Cultures & MASD

All wounds are contaminated. Not all are infected. Understanding this distinction guides your clinical response.

🛡️ Burn Infection Prevention — Full Precautions List

Infection is the leading cause of death in the acute phase of burn recovery. The destroyed skin barrier, immunosuppression from the burn injury, multiple IV lines, and prolonged hospitalization all create extreme infection risk.

Standard Precautions for Burn Patients

  • Strict hand hygiene — before and after all patient contact, even with gloves
  • Sterile dressing changes — full sterile technique, not just clean
  • Gown, mask, gloves, hair covers, shoe covers as required for wound care
  • Limit visitors — anyone with active illness must not enter; educate family on hand hygiene
  • No fresh flowers or plants — Pseudomonas and other organisms colonize soil and standing water
  • Sterile linens — for large TBSA burns

Additional Infection Considerations

  • Tetanus vaccination — update as needed; burn wounds are tetanus-prone
  • Cook fruits and vegetables rather than serving raw in high-risk settings — raw produce carries bacterial and fungal contamination
  • Monitor for systemic sepsis signs: fever OR hypothermia, tachycardia, tachypnea, elevated WBC or leukopenia, altered mental status, hemodynamic instability
  • Remember: SSD leukopenia can mimic sepsis — check timeline of SSD use before escalating
🔴
Burn sepsis is a leading cause of death in the acute phase. Any clinical deterioration in a burn patient must include sepsis in the differential — even without fever (hypothermia is common in late sepsis).
🦠 The Wound Infection Continuum — Why It Matters

Contamination

Microorganisms present on wound surface but not multiplying. No host response. Normal state for all wounds.

Colonization

Microorganisms multiplying in wound but no host tissue damage or immune response. Wound may have some odor. Still not "infected."

Critical Colonization

Bacterial burden begins impairing healing without frank infection signs. Wound fails to progress despite appropriate care. Biofilm likely forming.

Infection

Host tissue invasion with immune response. Local signs: REEDA (Redness, Edema, Ecchymosis, Drainage, Approximation disruption). Systemic: fever, leukocytosis, tachycardia → sepsis.

WHY this distinction matters: Colonization does not require systemic antibiotics. Critical colonization may respond to topical antimicrobials (silver dressings). True infection requires wound culture, targeted antibiotics, and increased assessment frequency. Treating colonization as infection leads to antibiotic overuse, C. diff, and resistance.
🔴 Signs of Wound Infection

Local Signs

  • Erythema spreading beyond wound margins — cellulitis; advancing edge = active bacterial invasion
  • Warmth — inflammatory mediators cause local vasodilation
  • Edema/Induration — fluid and leukocytes accumulating in tissue
  • Purulent drainage — neutrophil-rich exudate with bacteria and cellular debris
  • Foul odor — anaerobic bacterial metabolism produces sulfur compounds
  • Wound fails to progress despite appropriate care (bacterial burden overcoming healing)
  • Friable/bleeding granulation tissue — overgrowth with bacterial burden

Systemic Signs — Escalate Immediately

  • Fever >38°C (100.4°F) or hypothermia in severe sepsis (<36°C)
  • Elevated WBC (>12,000 cells/μL)
  • Tachycardia >100 bpm
  • Hypotension, ↓ urine output → septic shock
  • Altered mental status — perfusion to brain failing
  • ↑ respiratory rate, ↓ SpO₂
🚨
Report STAT: Crepitus (crackling under skin) = gas-producing organism = possible necrotizing fasciitis. This is a surgical emergency with mortality rates >20% if not treated within hours.
🧪 Wound Culture — Technique Matters
🔑
Always culture BEFORE antibiotics. Antibiotics within 24–72 hours of culture markedly suppress bacterial growth, producing false-negative results or misleading organisms. You'll be treating the wrong bug.

Levine Technique (Standard)

  • Gather: sterile swab/culture tube, wound cleanser, sterile gloves, gauze
  • Cleanse wound thoroughly first with normal saline or wound cleanser — remove surface slough, pus, old dressing residue
  • Allow to air dry briefly
  • Using sterile swab, press firmly and rotate over a 1 cm² area of viable wound tissue for 5 full seconds
  • Target: the healthiest-appearing tissue in the wound, NOT the pus or the necrotic tissue (those culture colonizers, not pathogens)
  • Insert swab into culture medium; label immediately (patient ID, wound site, date, time)
  • Transport within 30 minutes or per facility protocol
  • Document: wound appearance, specimen obtained, patient's current antibiotics (lab must know to interpret results)
WHY we cleanse first: Surface bacteria are colonizers, not invaders. The pathogens causing tissue damage are in the viable tissue. Culturing pus gives you whoever happens to be swimming in the debris, not necessarily what's destroying the tissue.

Labs to Monitor

  • CBC with differential — WBC trends (rising = worsening; normalizing = treatment working)
  • ESR, CRP — inflammatory markers; elevated and rising = ongoing infection
  • Procalcitonin — highly specific for bacterial infection; rising = escalate
  • Blood cultures if systemic signs present (fever, chills, hemodynamic instability)
  • BMP — renal function (guide antibiotic dosing); glucose (impairs immunity)
💦 Moisture-Associated Skin Damage (MASD) — Full Detail

A category of skin damage caused by prolonged exposure to various types of moisture. Critically different from pressure injuries but can co-occur and be confused with Stage 2 PI.

Incontinence-Associated Dermatitis (IAD)

Chemical injury from urease-producing bacteria in urine (producing ammonia) and digestive enzymes in stool. The skin's acid mantle (pH 5.5) is disrupted by alkaline urine and stool → keratinocyte breakdown → erosion → bacterial/fungal secondary infection.

  • Cleanse immediately after every incontinent episode with pH-neutral cleanser
  • Zinc oxide or antifungal/zinc cream TID and after each episode — forms physical barrier
  • Structured toileting program to reduce exposure duration
  • Fecal management system if severe/high-volume diarrhea
  • Key differentiator from Stage 2 PI: IAD distribution follows moisture path (diffuse, linear, along skin folds/perineum); Stage 2 PI is localized over a bony prominence

Skin Tears — Pathophysiology

Occurs when the mechanical bond between the epidermis and dermis is weaker than the shear force applied. In aging skin, the dermal-epidermal junction flattens (loss of rete pegs/dermal papillae), dramatically reducing the surface area of connection. A force that would not affect young skin tears the epidermis away from the dermis in elderly patients.

Skin Tear Management

  • Realign skin flap to original position (prevents desiccation of exposed dermis)
  • Steri-strips to hold aligned flap when appropriate
  • Non-adherent gauze + stretch gauze wrap — no adhesives directly on fragile skin
  • Sure Prep skin protectant under any tape that must be used
  • Remove ALL tape with adhesive remover lotion at low angle, slow peel
  • No Tegaderm on at-risk skin — creates more tears on removal than original wound
  • Prevention: long sleeves/pants for protection; gentle handling; padded side rails
Wound Closure & Advanced Therapy

Closure Types, NPWT & Debridement

🧵 Wound Closure — Intent Determines Method

Wound edges approximated immediately after injury or surgery. Best cosmetic outcome. Used for: clean surgical wounds, clean lacerations, incisions with minimal contamination.

  • Sutures: Absorbable (dissolve, used for deep layers and mucous membranes) or non-absorbable (removed when healed, used for skin). Match suture material to wound tension.
  • Staples: Quick, strong; used where appearance less critical (scalp, abdomen, orthopedic); removed based on location (scalp 7–10 days; abdomen 7–10 days; extremities 10–14 days)
  • Retention sutures: Large, heavy sutures placed to prevent dehiscence in high-risk patients (obese, malnourished, immunocompromised)
  • Skin glue/tissue adhesive (Dermabond): Superficial lacerations only; not under tension; not in areas with heavy moisture

Wound left open to heal by granulation, contraction, and epithelialization from the wound edges inward and upward. Used for: infected wounds, heavily contaminated wounds, pressure injuries, wounds with significant tissue loss. Slower, more scarring, but prevents trapping infection under a closed surface.

WHY: Closing an infected wound traps bacteria in a dark, warm, protein-rich environment with reduced immune surveillance — ideal conditions for abscess formation, sepsis, and wound dehiscence. Open wounds allow ongoing drainage, visualization, and treatment.

Wound left open initially to allow reduction of contamination/infection, then surgically closed 3–4 days later once clean. Used for traumatic wounds, war injuries, contaminated abdominal wounds. NPWT (Wound VAC) or moist gauze packing used in interim.

WHY wait 3–4 days: This is the inflammatory phase—macrophages are actively debridding debris and bacteria. By day 3–4, bacterial counts are typically reduced to safe levels and the wound is granulating, creating ideal conditions for successful closure.
🌀 Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (NPWT / Wound VAC)

Mechanism of Action

A foam sponge fills the wound; an occlusive drape creates a sealed environment; a vacuum pump applies sub-atmospheric pressure (typically –125 mmHg). This creates four simultaneous effects:

  • Removes exudate: Draws fluid, edema, and infectious material out of wound into canister
  • Promotes granulation: Mechanical stretch on cell membranes activates growth factor signaling → accelerates fibroblast proliferation and angiogenesis
  • Reduces bacterial load: Continuous removal of infected exudate; negative pressure reduces wound surface area
  • Wound contraction: Sub-atmospheric pressure mechanically draws wound edges together

Burns

Classification, Inhalation & Emergency Management

Burns are multisystem injuries. The visible wound is only one component. Airway, fluid balance, infection, and psychosocial recovery are equally critical.

🚨
Universal Burn Priority: ABCs first, always. The most dangerous burn complications in the first hours are airway obstruction and circulatory collapse—not the wound itself. A perfect wound dressing on a patient with an unrecognized inhalation injury means nothing.
🚑 Emergent/Resuscitative Phase — First 24–48 Hours

The 6 Goals of the Emergent Phase

  • 1. Secure airway / optimize oxygenation — intubate early if inhalation injury suspected; do not wait for stridor to develop
  • 2. Restore circulation / organ perfusion — Parkland LR resuscitation, target UO ≥30 mL/hr
  • 3. Manage pain aggressively — IV route only; titrate to effect
  • 4. Prevent infection — sterile wound care, isolation precautions, tetanus update
  • 5. Maintain body temperature — burned patients cannot thermoregulate; warm environment, warm IV fluids
  • 6. Psychosocial support — catastrophic injury; address fear, grief, and delirium from the first hours

Complete Inhalation Injury Sign List

Suspect inhalation injury with any enclosed-space fire. Airway edema can make intubation impossible once fully established — intubate EARLY.

  • Burns to face, neck, or oropharynx
  • Soot around mouth, nose, or in sputum
  • Singed nasal hair or eyebrows
  • Bloody, black, or carbonaceous sputum
  • Hoarseness — early laryngeal edema
  • Stridor — partial upper airway obstruction
  • Dry, brassy, or barking cough
  • Difficulty swallowing (dysphagia) or drooling
  • Accessory muscle use
  • Smoky smell on breath
  • Altered LOC — CO poisoning or hypoxia
☁️ Carbon Monoxide Poisoning — Assessment & Treatment

Why Pulse Ox Fails

CO binds hemoglobin 200–250× more avidly than O₂, forming carboxyhemoglobin (COHb). Standard pulse oximetry uses only 2 wavelengths and cannot distinguish COHb from OxyHb — SpO₂ reads falsely normal (96–99%) while the patient is cellular-level hypoxic.

Assessment

  • Order carboxyhemoglobin level (co-oximetry) — not standard pulse ox
  • Cherry-red mucous membranes — classic sign
  • Assess LOC — confusion, agitation, syncope = significant COHb
  • 12-lead ECG — CO causes arrhythmias and myocardial toxicity
  • Headache, nausea, visual changes — early symptoms

Treatment

  • 100% O₂ via non-rebreather mask immediately — reduces COHb half-life from ~5 hours (room air) to ~60 minutes
  • Continue high-flow O₂ for at least 6 hours
  • Hyperbaric oxygen (HBO) for severe poisoning (COHb >25%, LOC, cardiac involvement) — reduces half-life to ~20 min
⚠️
CO ≠ CO₂. Carbon monoxide (CO) is a colorless, odorless combustion gas. CO₂ is normal metabolic waste. Always document and communicate as carbon monoxide / carboxyhemoglobin.
🔴
Enclosed spaces = automatic CO concern. Never reassure based on SpO₂ alone. Order co-oximetry and treat empirically.
🌡️ Burn Classification — Depth & Clinical Correlation
Superficial

Epidermis only. Dry, red, painful, no blistering. Intact barrier function. Heals 3–5 days without treatment. Sunburn is the prototype. The superficial epidermis flakes off (peeling), replaced from basal layer.

Superficial Partial

Epidermis + superficial dermis. Most painful burn—abundant intact nerve endings. Wet, blistered, bright red/pink. Heals 10–21 days if no infection. Residual hair follicles and sweat glands survive to serve as re-epithelialization sources.

Deep Partial

Deep dermis destroyed. Less painful—fewer remaining nerve endings. Pale or mottled, may be dry, may blister. Hair follicles may still survive. Often requires grafting; heals with significant scarring in weeks if not grafted.

Full Thickness

All epidermis AND dermis destroyed. Painless—no nerve endings remain. Leathery, waxy, white/brown appearance. Eschar forms. Cannot regrow — requires grafting. No hair follicles or sweat glands survive.

Deep Full Thickness

Extends through skin into muscle, tendon, or bone. Charred appearance. May require amputation. Extensive reconstructive surgery required. All local structures destroyed.

⚠️
Pain Paradox — Exam Classic: Superficial partial thickness burns are the MOST PAINFUL. Full thickness burns are PAINLESS. Why? Nerve endings in the superficial dermis are exposed and hyperactivated in partial thickness. In full thickness, all nerve endings are destroyed—the patient reports no pain precisely because the injury is more severe. Never interpret painlessness as a reassuring sign in burns.

Sequential Emergency Actions

💨 Inhalation Injury — The Airway Threat
🚨
Inhalation injury is the leading cause of death in burn patients. Upper airway edema can progress from mild hoarseness to complete obstruction within HOURS. A patient who does not need intubation on arrival may become impossible to intubate 4 hours later as edema worsens. This is why burn providers have a low threshold for early prophylactic intubation.

Types of Inhalation Injury & Mechanisms

  • Direct thermal airway injury: Heat (especially steam) damages the oropharynx and supraglottic structures → mucosal edema → progressive airway narrowing. The lower airways are somewhat protected because the nasopharynx is an excellent heat exchanger.
  • Carbon monoxide poisoning: CO has 200–250× higher affinity for hemoglobin than O₂ → carboxyhemoglobin → functional anemia at tissue level despite normal SpO₂ reading.
  • Chemical smoke poisoning: Combustion produces aldehydes, hydrogen cyanide, and other toxins that directly damage bronchial mucosa → bronchospasm, bronchorrhea, mucosal sloughing.
  • Subglottic injury: Irritant chemicals (not heat) can reach lower airways → chemical pneumonitis → ARDS develops 24–72 hours post-injury.
  • External restriction: Tight circumferential chest eschar physically restricts thoracic expansion → restrictive respiratory failure → escharotomy needed.

Clinical Indicators — Assess ALL of These

  • Facial or neck burns — high-energy proximity to airway
  • Carbonaceous (black) sputum — particles of smoke in airway
  • Soot around nose, mouth, in nares
  • Singed nasal/facial/scalp hair — indicates proximity to flame heat
  • Hoarseness, stridor — supraglottic edema developing
  • Brassy or dry cough — mucosal irritation
  • Drooling — unable to swallow secretions (severe edema)
  • Difficulty swallowing
  • Accessory muscle use, intercostal retractions
  • Abnormal breath sounds; wheezing
  • "Smoky" smell to breath
  • Bloody sputum — mucosal erosion
🚨
Critical Exam Trap: Patient has wheezing on initial assessment → reassessment shows NO wheezing. This is NOT improvement — the airway is closing off. Absence of wheeze with history of inhalation injury = near-complete obstruction. ESCALATE IMMEDIATELY.

Evaluating Effective Airway Management

  • SpO₂ ≥95% (but check COHb if CO suspected — SpO₂ unreliable)
  • RR 12–20/min, unlabored
  • Clear bilateral breath sounds
  • Patient speaking without dyspnea or stridor
  • ABGs within acceptable range (PaO₂ ≥80 mmHg)
  • No accessory muscle use
💨 Carbon Monoxide Poisoning — Complete Clinical Picture

Pathophysiology

Carbon monoxide (CO) is a colorless, odorless gas produced by incomplete combustion. It binds hemoglobin at the same site as oxygen but with 200–250× greater affinity, forming carboxyhemoglobin (COHb). The Hgb that is bound to CO cannot carry O₂. Additionally, CO shifts the oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve LEFT — the hemoglobin that isn't bound to CO holds O₂ more tightly, releasing less to tissues. The result is cellular hypoxia with apparently normal hemoglobin levels.

WHY pulse oximetry fails: Standard pulse oximetry uses two wavelengths of light (660 nm red and 940 nm infrared) to distinguish oxyhemoglobin from deoxyhemoglobin. Carboxyhemoglobin absorbs light at 660 nm almost identically to oxyhemoglobin. The device literally cannot tell the difference — it reads COHb as if it were OHgb and reports a falsely normal saturation.

Cherry Red Mucous Membranes

CO causes peripheral vasodilation, flushing out pallor. Additionally, COHb has a bright cherry-red color rather than the blue of deoxyhemoglobin. The result: mucous membranes appear cherry red rather than cyanotic — the patient LOOKS well-oxygenated but is profoundly hypoxic at the tissue level.

Symptom Progression (by COHb level)

  • 10–20% COHb Headache, dizziness, nausea, impaired judgment
  • 20–40% COHb Confusion, drowsiness, tachycardia, vomiting
  • 40–60% COHb Cardiopulmonary instability, seizures, coma
  • >60% COHb Death

Diagnosis & Treatment

  • Stat carboxyhemoglobin (COHb) level via co-oximetry (not standard ABG unless co-oximeter available)
  • LOC assessment (Glasgow Coma Scale)
  • 12-lead EKG — CO causes myocardial toxicity and arrhythmias
  • 100% O₂ via non-rebreather for ≥6 hours — the half-life of COHb on room air is ~5 hours; on 100% O₂, it decreases to ~60–90 minutes. High-flow O₂ competitively displaces CO from hemoglobin.
  • Hyperbaric oxygen (HBO) for severe cases — allows dissolved O₂ to bypass hemoglobin entirely; reduces COHb half-life to ~20–30 min
  • Continuous cardiac monitoring
⚡ Electrical Burns — The "Grand Masquerader"

Called the "grand masquerader" and "tip of the iceberg" because the external wound dramatically underestimates the true injury. Electricity follows the path of least resistance through the body—primarily through blood vessels, nerves, and muscles (which are more conductive than bone or fat). The entry and exit wounds may be small burns, while massive destruction occurs along the entire conductive pathway.

WHY the iceberg analogy: A 1 cm entry wound on the hand with a 2 cm exit wound on the foot means electricity traveled through the entire arm, chest, and leg. Cardiac muscle, skeletal muscle, nerve tissue, and blood vessels throughout that path may all be damaged.

Critical Nursing Actions

  • ECG within minutes of arrival — cardiac arrhythmias (including V-fib) can be immediate or delayed
  • Continuous cardiac monitoring ≥24 hours
  • Treat as potential cervical spine injury if fall or blast occurred
  • Monitor urine color — myoglobin from massive muscle breakdown turns urine dark brown/tea-colored
  • Aggressive fluid resuscitation to prevent renal tubular obstruction from myoglobin (target UO 1–2 mL/kg/hr)
  • Serial CK levels — elevated and rising = ongoing rhabdomyolysis
  • Hourly compartment syndrome assessment — extremities tense, hard, pain with passive stretch → notify provider STAT
🔪 Escharotomy & Fasciotomy — Why Incisions Save Limbs

Why Circumferential Burns Require Escharotomy

Full-thickness circumferential burns create a rigid, inelastic eschar around the extremity or thorax. As underlying tissue edema develops (which is inevitable during fluid resuscitation), the eschar cannot expand. This creates a tourniquet effect:

  • Chest eschar: Cannot expand → thoracic cage fixed → patient cannot take adequate tidal volumes → respiratory failure despite patent airway
  • Extremity eschar: Compartment pressure rises → venous outflow obstructs first → venous congestion worsens edema → arterial inflow eventually stops → irreversible ischemia → limb loss without escharotomy
WHY escharotomy works: The eschar is dead, insensate tissue. Incising through it releases the tension without causing pain (painless in full thickness burn) and allows edematous tissue room to expand. The incision does NOT enter fascia (that's fasciotomy) unless compartment syndrome has already developed at the fascial level.

Assessment Before Escharotomy

  • Assess q1h: distal pulses (Doppler if not palpable), capillary refill, sensation, movement, pain with passive stretch
  • Hourly compartment pressure measurements in high-risk extremities
  • Compartment pressure ≥30 mmHg or within 20–30 mmHg of diastolic BP → notify surgeon
Critical Care

Burn Shock & Fluid Resuscitation

The first 48 hours in a major burn are dominated by massive fluid shifts. Understanding the physiology behind every parameter prevents both under-resuscitation (shock) and over-resuscitation (pulmonary edema).

⏱️ Phase Timeline — What's Happening When

Phase 1: Emergent/Resuscitative (0–48 hours)

Massive capillary hyperpermeability drives fluid, proteins, and electrolytes out of the vascular space into the interstitium (third spacing). Result: hypovolemia, hemoconcentration, edema, electrolyte shifts.

  • Fluid loss from vascular space → hypovolemic shock risk
  • Edema — visible in burn area AND remotely in large TBSA burns
  • Hyperkalemia — cell lysis releases intracellular K⁺
  • Hyponatremia — dilutional from fluid shifts
  • Hemoconcentration — elevated Hgb/Hct despite significant blood volume loss

Phase 2: Fluid Remobilization (48–72 hours)

Capillary integrity is restored. Third-spaced fluid returns to the vascular space — spontaneous diuresis begins. The danger now is hypervolemia and pulmonary edema if IV fluids are not reduced or stopped.

  • Hypokalemia — K⁺ returns to cells as fluid shifts back
  • Spontaneous diuresis — UO rises dramatically without fluid changes
  • Pulmonary edema risk — reduce IV rate, monitor lung sounds

Signs of Adequate Resuscitation — Know These Cold

✓
Primary indicator: UO ≥30 mL/hr (1–2 mL/kg/hr for electrical burns with myoglobinuria)

• SBP ≥90–100 mmHg
• HR trending toward 60–100
• Palpable peripheral pulses
• Near-normal serum sodium
• Intact mentation / improving LOC
• No worsening acidosis

Burn Shock

Burns cause a combined distributive + hypovolemic shock. Inflammatory mediators cause vasodilation (distributive) while third spacing removes intravascular volume (hypovolemic). Standard fluid resuscitation with Lactated Ringer's addresses both components.

💡
Timing starts at injury — not arrival. If a patient burned at 2:00 PM arrives at 5:00 PM, 3 hours of your 8-hour first-half window are already gone. Calculate the remaining time and adjust rate to still deliver the first half by 10:00 PM.
🌊 Pathophysiology of Burn Shock — The Two-Phase Fluid Shift

Phase 1 (0–48 hours): Fluid Escapes Vasculature

Burns trigger a massive systemic inflammatory response. Histamine, prostaglandins, bradykinin, and other mediators cause capillary hyperpermeability — the tight junctions between endothelial cells widen, allowing fluid, proteins, and electrolytes to leak into the interstitium. This is third spacing — fluid leaves the intravascular space but isn't excreted; it's sequestered in tissues where it's physiologically unavailable.

WHY hemoconcentration occurs: Fluid is leaving the plasma, but red blood cells and larger proteins can't follow (initially). The blood that remains in vessels has a higher proportion of cells relative to fluid → elevated hematocrit despite blood loss. This is NOT a reason to withhold fluids.
  • Hyperkalemia early: Massive cell destruction releases intracellular K⁺ into circulation. Monitoring critical — risk of cardiac dysrhythmias.
  • Hyponatremia: Sodium follows fluid into the interstitial space.
  • Burns >20–25% TBSA → response becomes SYSTEMIC (all capillaries throughout body become leaky, not just at burn site)

Phase 2 (48–72 hours): Fluid Remobilization

As inflammatory mediators begin to resolve, capillary integrity partially restores and third-spaced fluid is reabsorbed back into the vasculature. Spontaneous diuresis begins. This phase carries its own risk — rapid fluid return to circulation can cause circulatory overload and pulmonary edema.

  • Hypokalemia: K⁺ re-enters healing cells; K⁺ lost in diuresis
  • Continued hyponatremia: Dilutional effect from fluid mobilization
  • Nursing priority: Decrease IV fluid rates as UO ↑; monitor lungs for crackles; watch for HTN from fluid return
💡
Burn shock = COMBINED hypovolemic + distributive shock: Hypovolemic from third-spacing (not enough volume in vessels); distributive because widespread inflammatory vasodilation further reduces effective circulating volume. This combined mechanism is why fluid requirements are so massive.
🧮 Parkland Formula — Calculation & Administration
4 mL × kg × % TBSA
Lactated Ringer's — First 24 Hours Post-Burn

Administration Schedule — The WHY Behind Timing

  • First ½ given in first 8 HOURS POST-BURN (NOT post-arrival): The inflammatory response and capillary leak are most severe in the first 8 hours — this is when the body's demand for replacement fluid is greatest. If you discovered the burn 3 hours after it occurred, you adjust your rate calculation from hour 0 of injury, meaning the first 8-hour window has already started.
  • Second ½ given over next 16 hours: As capillary permeability begins to decrease, slower administration prevents fluid overload
  • Rates commonly exceed 125–150 mL/hr — this is expected, not excessive
  • Boluses are avoided: rapid volume infusion increases capillary hydrostatic pressure → worsens fluid leak into interstitium → more edema, not better resuscitation
  • Urine output is the primary titration tool — increase rate if UO falls; decrease if UO rises above target

Parameters Indicating Adequate Resuscitation

  • Urine output ≥30 mL/hr — most sensitive indicator of organ perfusion. Kidneys receive ~20% of cardiac output; adequate UO = adequate renal (and systemic) perfusion.
  • SBP ≥90–100 mmHg — minimum for adequate cerebral and coronary perfusion pressure
  • HR 60–100 bpm — persistent tachycardia suggests ongoing hypovolemia; monitor trends
  • Palpable peripheral pulses
  • Mental status intact — cerebral perfusion indicator
  • Near-normal serum sodium
⚠️
Myoglobinuria Exception: In electrical burns or rhabdomyolysis, target UO 1–2 mL/kg/hr (higher than standard) to flush myoglobin through renal tubules and prevent acute tubular necrosis. Dark brown/tea-colored urine = myoglobinuria = increase fluids and notify provider.
🚨
Mandatory Procedures for Major Burns: Foley catheter (can't accurately measure UO without one); large-bore IV ×2 through unburned skin if possible; NG tube for burns >20–25% TBSA (paralytic ileus risk → aspiration prevention); continuous cardiac monitor; Foley output documented hourly.
⚠️ Systemic Burn Complications — Pathophysiology & Management

Renal: Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN)

Hypovolemia during the resuscitative phase reduces renal perfusion → ischemic injury to proximal tubular cells. In electrical burns, myoglobin and hemoglobin released from destroyed muscle and RBCs precipitate in renal tubules → physical obstruction + direct toxicity → ATN. Monitor: BUN, creatinine, serum Na, urine color.

GI: Paralytic Ileus & Curling's Ulcer

Catecholamine surge from burn injury → splanchnic vasoconstriction → bowel ischemia → ileus. Insert NG to suction for burns >20–25% TBSA. Stress gastric ulcers (Curling's ulcer) develop from mucosal ischemia + ↑ gastric acid secretion. Prevent with PPI/H₂ blocker prophylaxis. Signs of GI bleeding: guaiac-positive stool, hematemesis, ↓ Hgb.

Infection & Sepsis — #1 Cause of Death in Acute Phase

The burn wound is an enormous protein-rich culture medium with impaired local immune function. The systemic inflammatory response from the burn itself can mimic sepsis (fever, tachycardia, leukocytosis), making true sepsis harder to detect. A change in mentation, worsening hemodynamics, or deteriorating wound appearance should prompt aggressive infectious workup.

Prevention Measures

  • Strict hand hygiene — the single most effective intervention
  • Gown, mask, hair cover, shoe covers for all wound contact
  • Sterile gloves for ALL dressing changes
  • Sterile linens; limit unnecessary visitors
  • No fresh flowers or plants (Pseudomonas found in standing water/soil)
  • Cooked fruits and vegetables only (raw produce harbors gram-negative organisms)
  • Verify tetanus immunization status on admission
Pain & Metabolic Support

Burn Pain Management & Nutritional Therapy

Burn pain is among the most severe forms of acute pain. Nutritional deficit kills wound healing capacity. Both require aggressive, evidence-based intervention.

💊 Burn Pain — Why IV is the Only Route in Acute Phase

In the emergent/acute burn phase, the entire physiology of drug absorption is disrupted. Third spacing and peripheral vasoconstriction mean:

  • Intramuscular (IM) route: Skeletal muscle blood flow is severely reduced; drug pools in muscle, is absorbed erratically and unpredictably. You could give a dose that appears to do nothing — then as perfusion restores, all the pooled drug absorbs at once → toxicity.
  • Subcutaneous (SQ) route: Subcutaneous blood flow is even more reduced; same problem as IM but worse
  • Oral route: Gastric motility is impaired (paralytic ileus); absorption from the GI tract is delayed and unpredictable. Vomiting risk in a patient with altered mental status.
  • Transdermal: Skin barrier is destroyed; absorption is uncontrolled and can lead to rapid systemic overdose
  • IV route: Directly enters circulation; predictable, controllable, instantly titratable, immediate onset. The ONLY reliable route in this physiologic state.

Types of Burn Pain

  • Background/Resting: Constant low-to-moderate pain from tissue injury and inflammation. Managed with scheduled (not PRN) baseline opioid dosing + adjuvants.
  • Procedural: Intense pain spikes during wound care, dressing changes, physical therapy. Requires pre-medication 30–45 min before procedure. Document whether pre-medication was adequate.
  • Breakthrough: Sudden, unpredicted pain spikes above baseline. Addressed with IV PRN dosing with clear reassessment time.

Role of Anxiety in Pain Perception

Anxiety and pain share overlapping neural pathways. Anxiolytic premedication before procedures (e.g., midazolam with fentanyl) reduces overall opioid requirement. Sleep deprivation — nearly universal in ICU burn patients — lowers pain thresholds and amplifies all pain experiences. Sleep restoration is an undervalued therapeutic priority.

Non-Pharmacological Adjuncts

  • Massage of non-burned areas → endorphin release; reduces systemic pain sensitization
  • Guided imagery, music therapy, virtual reality (emerging)
  • Distraction techniques during dressing changes — nurse talks, plays music, maintains eye contact
  • Proper positioning for comfort between procedures
  • Cold therapy: NOT used in burns — vasoconstriction worsens ischemia in already-damaged tissue; can deepen burn depth

Grafting Patient Pain Note

Donor sites (where skin was harvested) are often MORE painful than the graft recipient site. Why? The donor site is a large superficial partial-thickness wound with fully intact nerve endings. Set patient expectations and ensure robust donor site pain management.

😣 Pain Assessment — Objective & Behavioral Cues

Burn patients may be unable to verbally rate their pain due to altered mental status, intubation, sedation, developmental factors, or cultural barriers. The nurse must recognize non-verbal pain indicators and interpret vital signs in context.

Behavioral Indicators of Pain

  • Facial grimacing — furrowed brow, clenched jaw, tightened eyes during wound care or movement
  • Guarding — tensing or shielding the affected body part; refusing to let the nurse approach the wound
  • Restlessness / agitation — particularly in critically ill or sedated patients who cannot verbalize; agitation in burn ICU patients should trigger a pain assessment before assuming behavioral cause
  • Rigid posturing — extremity stiffness, fisted hands, extended or flexed limb positioning inconsistent with comfort
  • Vocalizations — moaning, crying, grunting during care activities even if the patient cannot speak or rate on a scale
  • Diaphoresis — sweating associated with painful stimuli can indicate breakthrough pain in sedated patients

Vital Signs in Clinical Context

Vital sign changes in burn patients are rarely caused by a single factor. Always interpret in context before acting.

  • Tachycardia — may reflect pain, anxiety, hypovolemia (under-resuscitation), fever/infection, or vasodilation from medications. Ask: is the rate new? Is it associated with a painful stimulus? Is the patient adequately fluid-resuscitated?
  • Elevated BP — pain and anxiety are primary causes in awake burn patients; also seen in Phase 2 fluid remobilization (over-resuscitation)
  • Respiratory changes — increased rate may indicate pain, anxiety, inhalation injury, or pulmonary edema
  • Hypotension — in the acute burn phase, first suspect under-resuscitation or septic shock, not pain management
🔑
Vital signs support the pain assessment — they do not replace it. An elevated heart rate that resolves after analgesia confirms pain as the cause. Always document both the objective cue AND the response to the intervention.
🍽️ Nutritional Therapy for Burns — The Metabolic Reality
🔑
Major burns are the most hypermetabolic state in clinical medicine. Basal metabolic rate may increase by 50–100% above normal. A patient with large burns may require >5,000 kcal/day — more than double the typical adult recommendation. Without aggressive nutritional support, the body catabolizes its own muscle mass for energy, further impairing healing, immunity, and functional recovery.

Macronutrient Requirements

  • Protein: 1.5–4 g/kg/day — required for collagen synthesis (wound repair), albumin production (osmotic pressure), immune globulin production, and preventing muscle wasting. The higher end is needed for large burns. Without adequate protein, the inflammatory phase of healing cannot transition to proliferation.
  • Carbohydrates: Primary caloric fuel; spare protein from being used for energy. Burns cause stress hyperglycemia (cortisol/glucagon response) — glucose management is essential because hyperglycemia impairs neutrophil function.
  • Lipids: Required for absorption of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) and for wound healing (arachidonic acid pathway). Immune function depends on essential fatty acids.

Route of Nutrition & Timing

  • Enteral (preferred) via nasoduodenal tube: Early enteral feeding (within 6 hours of burn) reduces gut mucosal atrophy, prevents bacterial translocation across intestinal wall (preventing gut-source sepsis), and has better metabolic outcomes than TPN. Nasoduodenal placement bypasses gastric ileus.
  • TPN = LAST RESORT: Invasive central venous access (infection risk in already-immunocompromised patient), expensive, doesn't maintain gut integrity, associated with more infectious and metabolic complications. Only used when enteral route completely unavailable.
  • Oral diet when GI tract functional and patient can tolerate; encourage high-protein, high-calorie foods

Key Supplements

  • Vitamin C: Essential cofactor for prolyl hydroxylase — the enzyme that hydroxylates proline to form hydroxyproline, the critical component of collagen. Without Vit C, collagen structure is unstable (this is why scurvy causes wound reopening).
  • Zinc: Cofactor for >300 enzymes; essential for DNA synthesis, cell division, and protein synthesis. Zinc deficiency delays wound healing significantly.
  • Multivitamin daily — hospitalized burn patients are uniformly deficient in multiple micronutrients

Grafting — Types & Nursing Post-Op Care

  • Autograft: Patient's own skin harvested from unburned donor site (thigh, back). Gold standard. Split-thickness (epidermis + partial dermis) or full-thickness (entire dermis). Full-thickness grafts = better cosmesis, more limited supply.
  • Sheet graft: Applied as intact piece; better cosmetic result; used for face/hands
  • Mesh graft: Sheet graft is cut in grid pattern and expanded to cover larger area; more scarring but covers more burn with less donor tissue
  • Cultured epithelium: Small skin biopsy cultured in lab over 2–3 weeks to grow large sheet; used when donor sites are inadequate (massive burns)
  • Artificial skin (Integra): Bilaminate scaffold provides dermal substrate; covered with ultra-thin autograft after 2–3 weeks of vascularization

Post-Graft Nursing Care

  • Immobilize grafted extremity completely — shear destroys new capillary buds forming between graft and wound bed. One premature movement can cause graft failure.
  • Assess graft adherence and appearance (graft should become pink/red as it vascularizes)
  • Graft sloughing = local failure; assess for underlying hematoma or infection
  • Donor site: painful superficial partial-thickness wound — requires non-adherent dressing; significant pain management needed
  • Elevate grafted extremity to ↓ venous congestion → ↓ edema under graft
  • Monitor for signs of graft infection (purulence under graft, foul odor, edges lifting)
Burn Recovery

Nutrition in Burn Recovery

Burns create one of the most extreme metabolic demands in medicine. Nutritional support is not supplemental — it is life-sustaining and directly determines whether wounds close or stall.

🥗 Hypermetabolic State — Why Burns Demand Aggressive Nutrition

What Happens Metabolically

Burns trigger a hypermetabolic state — the body's metabolic rate increases 50–100% above baseline in large burns. Protein catabolism accelerates to fuel the immune response, inflammatory mediator production, and wound repair. Without aggressive nutritional support, the body cannibalizes muscle (including cardiac and respiratory muscle), healing stalls, infection risk soars, and multi-organ failure follows.

Caloric & Protein Targets

  • Calories: May exceed 5,000 kcal/day in large burns — far above what any standard hospital tray provides
  • Protein: 1.5–4 g/kg/day (normal adult = 0.8 g/kg/day) — needed for collagen synthesis, immune function, and enzyme production
  • Vitamin C: Cofactor for prolyl hydroxylase — the enzyme that crosslinks collagen fibers. Without adequate Vitamin C, new collagen is fragile and disorganized
  • Zinc: Cofactor for 300+ enzymes involved in cell division and protein synthesis — essential for every phase of wound healing
⚠️
Albumin is a key nutrition marker. Albumin <3.5 g/dL = hypoalbuminemia → impaired healing, increased infection risk, and pressure injury development. Always assess in complex wound patients.

Route of Nutrition — Order of Preference

  • Enteral feeding is first choice — start within 6 hours of injury if GI tract is functional
  • Use nasoduodenal tube over nasogastric in patients with ileus or altered LOC — reduces aspiration risk
  • Early enteral feeding preserves gut mucosal integrity → prevents bacterial translocation (gut bacteria crossing into the bloodstream, causing systemic sepsis)
  • TPN is last resort — only when GI tract is completely nonfunctional. TPN carries central line infection risk and does not maintain gut mucosal health

Why Not Oral First?

In the emergent phase, most burn patients have a paralytic ileus (gut motility ceases due to the systemic inflammatory response), making oral intake unsafe. Even when oral feeding resumes later, caloric targets are so high that supplemental enteral tube feeds are usually required alongside oral intake.

💡
Enteral > TPN always when possible. "If the gut works, use it." This principle prevents bacterial translocation, maintains gut-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT) immune function, and reduces central line complications.
📊 Nutritional Assessment & Monitoring

Labs to Monitor

  • Albumin — half-life 20 days; reflects chronic nutritional status
  • Prealbumin — half-life 2–3 days; more sensitive to acute changes in nutritional status
  • Transferrin — intermediate marker; affected by iron status
  • BUN/Creatinine — elevated in catabolism; monitor kidney function during high-protein feeds
  • Blood glucose — hyperglycemia common from stress response; impairs neutrophil function and wound healing

Clinical Indicators of Nutritional Support Working

  • Wound progressing through healing phases (not stalled in inflammation)
  • Improving prealbumin trend
  • Stable or improving weight (accounting for fluid shifts)
  • No new infections or wound breakdown
  • Patient tolerating tube feeds without high residuals or aspiration

Dietitian Role

Burn nutrition is a specialty. A registered dietitian should be involved from day 1 in any significant burn. The nurse's role is to ensure tube feeds are infusing at the ordered rate, to report intolerance (high gastric residuals, vomiting, diarrhea), and to document oral intake accurately.

🔑
Early dietitian consult + early enteral feeding = the two most impactful nutritional interventions in burn care.
Rehabilitative Phase

Burn Rehabilitation & Continuity of Care

Rehabilitation begins in the emergent phase and may continue for years. The goal is the highest possible quality of life — not just wound closure.

🌱 Rehabilitation Phase — Priorities After Stabilization

When the Rehab Phase Begins

Once ABCs are controlled and early survival is secured, focus shifts to wound closure, function restoration, and quality of life. This phase may last months to years and often overlaps with the acute phase — early splinting and ROM exercises begin as soon as the patient is hemodynamically stable.

Physical Rehab Goals

  • Wound closure/healing — grafting completion, donor site healing
  • Scar prevention — pressure garments 23 hr/day, silicone gel, massage
  • Contracture prevention — daily ROM, splinting in functional position, early mobilization
  • Skin lubrication — q4–6h with fragrance-free emollient; healed burn skin has no sweat or sebaceous glands
  • Sun protection — SPF 50+ indefinitely; healed burn skin has no melanin protection and burns easily

Psychosocial & Functional Recovery

  • PTSD screening — affects up to 45% of burn survivors; early referral to mental health
  • Vocational rehabilitation — return to work, adaptive equipment, job retraining
  • Thermoregulation counseling — burned areas cannot sweat; patients must avoid overheating environments and use external cooling
  • Pruritus management — severe itching is common as nerves regenerate; antihistamines, moisturizers, cold packs
  • Support groups and counseling — body image, survivor guilt, relationship changes
  • Reconstructive surgery may be needed — deferred until scars mature (~1–2 years)
💡
Recovery is not linear. Hypertrophic scar maturation takes 12–24 months. Pressure garments must be worn for the full duration even when the patient feels they're healed. Compliance education is a major nursing role.
🌟 Hypertrophic Scarring — Mechanism & Prevention

After full-thickness burns, healing occurs by scar tissue formation rather than true skin regeneration. Fibroblasts produce disorganized, excessive collagen — this forms raised, hypertrophic scars that continue to contract over months to years. Scar contracture across joints leads to permanent loss of range of motion (contracture) if not prevented aggressively.

WHY elastic pressure garments work: External mechanical pressure applied at 25–30 mmHg (capillary closing pressure) reduces collagen fibril synthesis, reorients collagen fibers, reduces scar vascularity, and flattens the scar. They must be worn 23 hours/day for 12–24 months because scar remodeling continues for up to 2 years post-injury. Removing them even briefly allows the scar to thicken again.

Scar Management Interventions

  • Elastic pressure garments — custom-fitted; begin when wounds >50% closed; worn until scar matures (pale, flat, supple)
  • Silicone gel sheets or cream — applied under or in addition to pressure garments; ↓ scar thickness via hydration and temperature effect on collagen synthesis
  • Intralesional corticosteroid injections — for persistent hypertrophic areas
  • Splinting in functional position — prevents contracture across joints; especially hands, neck, axilla, antecubital fossa
  • Active ROM exercises from earliest possible time in emergent phase
  • Stretch garments, range of motion, and splints work together — cannot do just one

Discharge Planning — Comprehensive Needs Assessment

  • Skin care regimen: Keep healed burn skin lubricated q4–6h minimum; healed scars have no sebaceous glands; dryness → cracking → wound recurrence
  • Sun protection: Healed burn skin has no melanin protection and is hypersensitive to UV → SPF 50+ daily; protective clothing
  • Thermoregulation: Burned areas cannot sweat → no evaporative cooling → hyperthermia risk. Dress in layers; avoid prolonged heat/cold exposure.
  • Pruritus management: Scar pruritus is intense and poorly understood; antihistamines, gabapentin, topical doxepin, massage
  • Return to work/school: Vocational rehabilitation referral if function is limited
  • Sexual concerns: Valid and common; address matter-of-factly; sexual function, altered body image, partner communication
  • PTSD screening: Up to 45% of burn survivors develop PTSD; early mental health referral is standard of care
  • Support groups: Phoenix Society for Burn Survivors; online communities

Pharmacology

Medications, Mechanisms & Nursing Considerations

Every medication here appears in your lecture notes. Know the mechanism, the clinical context, and when to hold.

🧴 Topical Wound Agents

Silver Sulfadiazine (Silvadene)
Topical Antimicrobial · Sulfonamide Derivative
Mechanism

Silver ions denature bacterial DNA and disrupt cell membrane integrity; broad-spectrum coverage including Pseudomonas aeruginosa (the #1 burn wound pathogen), gram-positive and gram-negative organisms

Clinical Context

Standard prophylactic and therapeutic agent for partial and full thickness burns. Applied as 1–4 mm cream layer to wound bed.

Expected Effect

Reduced wound bacterial burden → prevents infection progression; maintains moist wound environment conducive to healing

Side Effects

Transient leukopenia (most important) — silver-sulfadiazine can suppress bone marrow WBC production. Usually mild and reversible. Monitor CBC regularly. Skin discoloration (gray-brown).

Contraindications

Sulfonamide/sulfa allergy; near-term pregnancy; newborns (risk of kernicterus from sulfa displacement of bilirubin from albumin); G6PD deficiency

Nursing Actions

Remove old cream before each new application (residue inhibits fresh silver delivery); apply with sterile-gloved hand; cover with dressing; monitor WBC with prolonged use

⚠️
Hold and Notify: WBC <3,000/μL; signs of sulfa allergy (rash, fever, angioedema); worsening wound despite application (consider organism resistance)
Lidocaine Spray (Topical)
Local Anesthetic · Amide Class
Mechanism

Reversibly blocks voltage-gated sodium channels in nerve membranes → prevents action potential generation and propagation → temporary anesthesia at application site

Clinical Context

Superficial/superficial partial burns; wound dressing changes; procedural analgesia for minor painful procedures. Provides 10–30 min of localized numbness.

Toxicity Progression

With excess systemic absorption: tinnitus → metallic taste → circumoral numbness → visual changes → confusion → seizures → cardiovascular collapse (Na channel blockade in cardiac cells)

Contraindications

Amide anesthetic allergy; large wound surface area (risk of systemic absorption exceeding safe dose); hepatic failure (↓ lidocaine metabolism)

Nursing Monitoring

Apply minimum dose for coverage; monitor for CNS/cardiovascular systemic signs especially on large areas; assess effectiveness of pain reduction

🚨
Toxicity Red Flags — STOP Immediately: Tinnitus, metallic taste, circumoral tingling, confusion, tremors, bradycardia, hypotension, seizures. Treatment: airway support, benzodiazepines for seizures, cardiac monitoring, lipid emulsion therapy for severe toxicity.
Aloe Vera (Topical)
Botanical · Adjunct Wound Care
Mechanism

Acemannan (polysaccharide) activates macrophages; bradykininase enzyme degrades bradykinin (a pain mediator); anti-inflammatory effects via prostaglandin inhibition; antimicrobial activity against some organisms

Clinical Context

Superficial and superficial partial thickness burns. Adjunct, not primary therapy. Reduces inflammation, provides cooling sensation, maintains moist environment.

Teaching Points

Use pure aloe vera (no alcohol, fragrance, or colorants — these are irritating and counterproductive). Monitor for contact allergic reaction (rare). Not a substitute for medical evaluation for deeper burns.

💊 Opioid Analgesics — Burn Pain

Morphine Sulfate IV
Opioid Analgesic · Mu, Kappa, Delta Receptor Full Agonist
Mechanism

Binds opioid receptors in CNS (dorsal horn, thalamus, limbic system) → inhibits adenylyl cyclase → ↓ cAMP → hyperpolarization of neurons → decreased pain signal transmission AND decreased emotional response to pain

Burn Context

Background pain management, procedural premedication. IV-only route in acute phase (see pharmacokinetics rationale). PCA may be appropriate when patient is coherent.

Expected Effect

NRS pain score ↓ ≥2 points, patient reports tolerable pain, able to participate in wound care, HR/RR normalizing, decreased agitation

Side Effects

Respiratory depression (priority!), constipation, nausea/vomiting, sedation, urinary retention, pruritus (histamine release), hypotension, miosis (pinpoint pupils)

Pre-Admin Assessment

RR ≥12/min; SpO₂ adequate; BP ≥90 mmHg; LOC baseline; pain score; allergy verification; last dose timing; sedation scale

Monitoring

RR q15–30 min after each dose; SpO₂ continuous; sedation scale; BP; pain score reassessment in 30 min; bowel sounds; urine output

🚨
Hold Parameters — Hold and Notify Provider: RR <12/min; SpO₂ <92%; RASS sedation score ≥+2 (too agitated) or ≤-3 (too sedated); pinpoint pupils with altered LOC. Naloxone (Narcan) must be at bedside for all patients receiving opioids. Naloxone dose: 0.4–2 mg IV; may repeat q2–3 min; duration shorter than opioids — monitor for re-narcotization.
Hydromorphone (Dilaudid) IV
Opioid Analgesic · More Potent Than Morphine (5–10×)
Why Choose Over Morphine?

Less histamine release (less pruritus); faster onset; no active metabolites accumulating in renal failure (morphine's M6G metabolite is renally excreted and can accumulate); more predictable in hepatic/renal impairment

Potency Context

5–10× more potent than morphine — meaning IV doses are in the 0.2–0.8 mg range vs. morphine 2–4 mg. Dosing errors are more consequential. Calculate carefully.

Side Effects & Hold Parameters

Identical to morphine: respiratory depression, sedation, hypotension. Same hold parameters: RR <12, SpO₂ <92%, excessive sedation. Naloxone at bedside.

Fentanyl IV / PCA
Opioid Analgesic · 100× More Potent Than Morphine · Short-Acting
Mechanism Advantage

Highly lipophilic → very rapid CNS penetration → onset within 1–2 min IV. Duration 30–60 min IV. This makes it ideal for procedural pain: maximum analgesia during the procedure, then cleared before prolonged sedation.

Burn Context

Best for procedural pain (dressing changes, wound debridement, PT). Also used as continuous infusion in mechanically ventilated patients.

Unique Side Effect

Chest wall rigidity ("wooden chest") with rapid high-dose IV push — thoracic muscles become rigid, impeding ventilation. Treated with naloxone or neuromuscular blockade + intubation in severe cases.

Monitoring

Continuous SpO₂ during infusion; RR; BP; sedation scale; do NOT give rapid IV push of large doses in awake patients

🚨
Chest wall rigidity: Patient becomes unable to ventilate despite appearing awake. Unique to fentanyl with rapid IV push. Immediate action: naloxone IV; if no response, succinylcholine + intubation.
Ketamine IV
Dissociative Anesthetic · NMDA Receptor Antagonist
Mechanism

Blocks NMDA (glutamate) receptors → dissociative anesthesia: patient is in a "dissociated" state (eyes may be open, reflexes intact) but unaware of and unresponsive to painful stimuli. Also has opioid receptor activity and is a potent bronchodilator.

Unique Advantages in Burns

Maintains airway reflexes (less aspiration risk than other anesthetics); causes bronchodilation (helpful in inhalation injury patients); maintains hemodynamic stability; allows rapid recovery. Ideal for burn dressing changes.

Side Effects

Emergence reactions: vivid hallucinations, nightmares, agitation during recovery. Give benzodiazepine (midazolam) with ketamine to reduce this. Increased secretions (have suction available). Tachycardia/HTN (from catecholamine release).

Monitoring

HR, BP, secretion management, respiratory rate, emergence reactions during recovery. Recovery environment should be calm, lights dimmed, minimal stimulation during emergence.

💊 Non-Opioid Analgesics

Acetaminophen (Tylenol)
Non-Opioid Analgesic · Antipyretic
Mechanism

Inhibits central prostaglandin synthesis (COX inhibition in CNS and endocannabinoid system modulation). No significant peripheral anti-inflammatory effect (unlike NSAIDs), explaining its lack of GI side effects and platelet effects.

Burn Context

Scheduled background analgesia; antipyretic for burn fever; opioid-sparing effect (reduces total opioid requirement by 20–30%); appropriate for superficial burn pain. Should be given on a schedule, not PRN, for chronic pain management.

Hepatotoxicity

Converted to toxic metabolite NAPQI by CYP450 enzymes. Normally detoxified by glutathione. In overdose, fasting, or chronic alcohol use, glutathione depleted → NAPQI accumulates → hepatocyte necrosis. Treatment: N-acetylcysteine (NAC) repletes glutathione stores.

Dose Limits

≤4 g/day in healthy adults; ≤3 g/day in elderly, malnourished, or liver disease patients; ≤2 g/day in chronic alcohol use. Account for all sources (combination products).

Toxicity Signs

RUQ pain, nausea, malaise (24–72h after overdose); then jaundice, elevated LFTs, coagulopathy; then acute liver failure if untreated

Ibuprofen (Motrin/Advil)
NSAID · COX-1 and COX-2 Non-Selective Inhibitor
Mechanism

Inhibits cyclooxygenase enzymes (COX-1 and COX-2) → ↓ prostaglandin synthesis → analgesia + anti-inflammatory + antipyretic effects. COX-1 inhibition also suppresses thromboxane A2 → reduced platelet aggregation → bleeding risk.

Burn Context

Superficial/superficial partial burns; mild–moderate pain; anti-inflammatory effect beneficial in early burn edema. Combined with acetaminophen for synergistic effect (different mechanisms).

Renal Concern (Critical in Burns)

Prostaglandins maintain renal afferent arteriolar tone when systemic perfusion is reduced. NSAIDs block prostaglandins → afferent arteriole constricts → ↓ GFR → acute kidney injury. In burn patients who are volume-depleted, this risk is dramatically elevated.

GI Risk

COX-1 produces prostaglandins that protect gastric mucosa. Inhibition → reduced mucus production, reduced bicarbonate secretion, reduced mucosal blood flow → gastric ulceration. Give with food; consider PPI co-prescription in high-risk patients.

Hold Criteria

Renal impairment (↑ creatinine); volume depletion; active GI bleeding; severe hepatic disease; concurrent anticoagulation (↑ bleeding risk); third trimester pregnancy (premature ductus closure)

⚠️
Caution in Burn Patients: NSAIDs should be used cautiously in major burns due to: volume depletion (renal failure risk), stress ulcer risk (already elevated from burn), platelet inhibition (bleeding risk during surgical procedures). Benefit vs. risk must be assessed individually.
Clinical Reasoning

NCLEX Traps & Priority Clinical Decisions

The most commonly tested distinctions in tissue integrity — tested as both direct knowledge and clinical reasoning.

⚡ Know These Cold — High-Yield Quick Reference

Staging & Wounds

  • Only pressure injuries are staged — not venous, arterial, or diabetic ulcers
  • Blanchable redness = reactive hyperemia, NOT a pressure injury — remove pressure and moisturize
  • Non-blanchable erythema over bony prominence = Stage 1 — tissue damage has begun
  • Purple/maroon discoloration = suspect Deep Tissue Injury — may look minor but is severe underneath
  • Never downstage — scar tissue ≠ regenerated dermis; a healed Stage 4 stays documented as Stage 4
  • Unstageable = wound base covered by slough or eschar; cannot determine depth

Dressing Selection

  • Dry wound → add moisture → hydrogel / wound gel
  • Wet/heavy drainage → absorb → calcium alginate
  • Partial thickness, low-moderate drainage → hydrocolloid or foam
  • Poorly perfused wound (ischemic) → keep DRY — moist healing exception
  • Compression requires ABI verification before applying — ABI <0.6 = contraindicated

Burns

  • Burns priorities = ABCs first, always
  • Inhalation injury signs: facial burns, soot, singed hairs, hoarseness, stridor, carbonaceous sputum
  • Disappearing wheeze = worsening — airway narrowed below wheeze threshold; escalate immediately
  • CO poisoning: pulse ox UNRELIABLE — order carboxyhemoglobin, apply 100% O₂ ×6 hours
  • Successful fluid resuscitation = UO ≥30 mL/hr, stable BP, improving mentation
  • Parkland formula: 4 mL × kg × %TBSA — clock starts at injury time, not arrival
  • IV route only for burn pain medications — no IM, oral, or transdermal
  • Severe burns: calorie needs may exceed 5,000 kcal/day; protein 1.5–4 g/kg/day
  • Enteral feeding preferred — start within 6 hours if GI tract is functional

Wound Emergencies

  • Evisceration: cover with sterile saline-moistened dressing FIRST — never push organs back
  • Escharotomy: circumferential eschar → compartment syndrome → impaired distal perfusion → incise eschar
  • Crepitus: gas-producing organism → necrotizing fasciitis → surgical emergency
🎯 Staging Traps

Blanchable = reactive hyperemia = NOT a pressure injury. The key test: press firmly with one finger and release. If the redness turns white (blanches) = capillary bed intact = reactive hyperemia = normal vascular response = no stage.

Non-blanchable = Stage 1. The redness does not change with pressure = capillary bed is damaged and can no longer respond to pressure = tissue injury has begun.

🚨
Exam questions often give you a red area and ask you to "stage" it. Before assigning Stage 1, confirm it's non-blanchable. If blanchable, the correct documentation is "reactive hyperemia" or "blanchable erythema" — NOT Stage 1.

A bruise-like DTI on the surface of an elderly patient's heel might be the only visible sign of a wound that has destroyed 3–4 cm of subcutaneous tissue and approaches muscle. The surface appearance is to the true injury as the tip of an iceberg is to the mass below waterline.

Key physical assessment sign: boggy or mushy feel under an area of discoloration = DTI until proven otherwise. Normal tissue feels firm. Compromised subcutaneous/muscle tissue feels like it's filled with fluid or has lost structural integrity.

DTI can evolve to expose Stage 3 or 4 injury within days. Document carefully; photograph. Never tell the patient or family this is a new injury caused by nursing care when it was pre-existing.

A Stage 4 that is actively healing is still documented as "Healing Stage 4" — it never becomes Stage 3, then 2, then 1 as it heals. The stage indicates the maximum depth of tissue damage that occurred. The tissue filling the wound is scar tissue, not regenerated dermis — the tissue at the lower stages never truly returns.

NCLEX will ask you to stage a wound that is "healing nicely" and was previously Stage 4. Correct answer: Still Stage 4.

💧 Wound Product Traps

Calcium Alginate = WET wounds (moderate–heavy exudate)
Alginate is a highly absorptive material derived from seaweed. It draws up to 20× its weight in fluid. Using it in a dry wound would desiccate the wound bed, damaging new epithelial cells.

Hydrogel/Wound Gel = DRY wounds (minimal to no exudate)
Hydrogel donates moisture. Using it in a wet wound would add moisture to an already over-wet environment, causing maceration of wound edges and periwound skin.

🚨
Swapping these products actively worsens the wound. NCLEX questions will give you wound characteristics (wet vs. dry) and ask which dressing. Base your answer on the moisture balance rule.

Compression stockings and wraps are therapeutic for venous disease — they reduce venous hypertension by augmenting venous return. However, in arterial disease, compression adds external pressure on top of already-insufficient arterial inflow. The already barely-adequate arterial pressure cannot overcome both gravity AND external compression → tissue ischemia → rapid wound extension → potential amputation.

Always verify ABI before applying any leg compression. ABI <0.6 = compression contraindicated; ABI 0.6–0.8 = modified compression only; ABI >0.8 = compression safe for venous disease.

The one situation where moist wound healing is contraindicated is a poorly perfused wound (heel eschar in PAD, ischemic toes). Without blood flow, there are no neutrophils to fight bacteria and no macrophages to coordinate healing. A moist, warm wound environment simply creates ideal bacterial culture conditions without any defense mechanism.

Stable, intact, dry heel eschar in ischemic limb: keep it dry and intact. It is serving as a biological barrier. Monitor for signs of infection: fluctuance, erythema, drainage, odor. If those develop → debridement + revascularization consult needed.

🔥 Burn Priority Traps

A burn patient arrives from a house fire. SpO₂ is 98%. Cherry-red mucous membranes. The nurse is NOT reassured by the SpO₂. Standard pulse oximetry uses spectrophotometry at 660 nm and 940 nm wavelengths — carboxyhemoglobin absorbs light nearly identically to oxyhemoglobin at these wavelengths. The device cannot distinguish them and reports a falsely elevated saturation.

The ONLY way to detect CO poisoning is: (1) co-oximetry (measures COHb directly), (2) carboxyhemoglobin level from ABG lab ordered specifically.

NCLEX: Do NOT trust SpO₂ in enclosed-space smoke inhalation. Order carboxyhemoglobin. Treat with 100% O₂ ×6 hours regardless.

This is one of the most counter-intuitive traps in burn nursing. A patient with inhalation injury is wheezing on assessment — this indicates some airway patency (air is moving and turbulently enough to produce sound). On reassessment, the wheeze is gone. Many students think this is improvement.

Absent wheeze with worsening respiratory effort = impending complete airway obstruction. The airway has narrowed to the point that there is insufficient airflow to generate a wheeze — the patient is about to obstruct completely. This is a respiratory emergency requiring immediate escalation and likely emergency intubation.

The clinical picture that confirms worsening: still labored breathing, increasing use of accessory muscles, worsening stridor, decreased SpO₂, increasing anxiety.

A patient with a full thickness burn says they feel no pain in the burned area. A student nurse is reassured. Do NOT be reassured.

Full thickness burns destroy all nerve endings in the dermis. The absence of pain is a sign of the MOST SEVERE burn depth, not a sign of a mild injury. In contrast, a patient with a superficial partial-thickness burn (less severe) will be in severe pain because all nerve endings are intact and exposed.

Pain level is INVERSELY related to burn depth in the burn depth context specifically: more severe burn = less pain (at the wound site). Patients with full thickness burns may have pain from surrounding partial thickness areas, so assessment must include the entire wound field.

Scope of Practice

Delegation — AP, LPN, & RN Scope

Delegation questions appear on virtually every nursing exam. Know the lines clearly — and the WHY behind them.

🔑
Delegation Principles: The RN is always accountable — delegation transfers the task, not the responsibility. Delegate only to someone competent to perform the task safely. Never delegate assessment, judgment, teaching initiation, or evaluation — these require the nursing process and belong to the RN.
👤 Assistive Personnel (AP/CNA)

Non-invasive tasks that require no clinical judgment, only technique. Patient must be stable.

  • Basic skin care and hygiene (bathing, perineal care)
  • Ambulation (after RN evaluation confirms it's safe)
  • Positioning (with specific RN guidance on restrictions)
  • Range-of-motion exercises (routine, non-complex)
  • Vital signs on stable patients
  • Specimen collection (urine/stool — basic)
  • Meal assistance (no aspiration risk patients)
  • Client transport
  • Grooming
⚠️
AP CANNOT: assess wounds, make clinical judgments, teach, administer ANY medications, or perform any invasive procedures.
🩺 LPN / LVN

All AP tasks plus invasive but non-complex nursing tasks. Data collection (not assessment), implementing (not planning).

  • Changing dressings (routine; stable wound)
  • Irrigating wounds
  • Oral medications
  • IM, SQ, ID, topical, rectal, vaginal, eye/ear/nose, GT medications
  • Selected IV piggyback medications (per facility policy)
  • Monitoring IV flow rates
  • Suctioning
  • Urinary catheterization
  • Teaching basic hygienic/nutritional measures (reinforcement only)
  • Ongoing data collection and implementing established care plan
⚠️
LPN CANNOT: administer IV push medications, perform initial assessments, initiate client teaching, or make care plan decisions.
👩‍⚕️ Registered Nurse (RN) — RN Only

Full nursing process; complex and unstable patients; initiation of care; judgment and evaluation.

  • Initiating client teaching (critical content)
  • IV push medications (morphine, hydromorphone, fentanyl, etc.)
  • Continuous IV infusions
  • Initial and ongoing nursing assessment
  • Nursing diagnoses, care planning, evaluation
  • Complex wound assessment and management
  • Emergency nursing interventions
  • Notifying providers of critical changes
  • Supervising and evaluating delegated tasks
  • Admission assessment and discharge planning
💡
The Teaching Rule: "Initiating client teaching" is always RN. The LPN can reinforce simple, basic hygienic or nutritional information previously taught by the RN, but cannot design or initiate a teaching plan. On NCLEX, any answer involving "teach the patient about [complex topic]" as a delegation option to LPN is incorrect.
